**Bottom line**

The most conservative Polish answer is: **do not claim a stand-alone PIT-38 “loss” just because Celsius or BlockFi froze withdrawals or entered bankruptcy**. Under the current Polish PIT rules, crypto is taxed only on **odpłatne zbycie**—exchange into fiat, goods, services, a non-crypto property right, or settlement of a liability—and the Ministry of Finance states that a **crypto tax loss does not arise**. What survives instead are **documented acquisition/disposal costs**, reported in PIT-38 and rolled forward to later years if unused; those costs offset only **crypto-disposal income**, not salary or ordinary business income. ([Podatki][1])

I did not find a Polish PIT interpretation specifically on **Celsius** or **BlockFi** private-user bankruptcies. The clear parts come from the statute and official MF guidance; the uncertain parts are **(i)** whether the deposit product was pure custody or a lending/earn product, and **(ii)** how any later court-plan recovery should be classified.

**1. Can losses be claimed?**

Not as a separate deductible **“bankruptcy loss”** on PIT-38. The legal basis is the combination of **art. 17 ust. 1f**, **art. 22 ust. 14–16**, **art. 30b ust. 1a, 1b, 5d, 6a**, and **art. 9 ust. 3a pkt 2** ustawy PIT: Poland allows documented purchase/disposal costs and carryforward of unused costs, but **not** normal five-year loss relief for crypto. So the economic relief, if any, comes from preserving the **original purchase cost** in the crypto cost pool; it is **not** a separate deduction against general income. ([Podatki][2])

**2. When does the “loss” occur for Polish tax purposes?**

None of the dates you listed—withdrawal freeze, bankruptcy petition, or court acknowledgment—is a clean statutory **loss trigger** under Polish PIT, because the statute cares about **disposal**, not about platform solvency. In practice, the strongest evidentiary moment for permanent non-recovery is a later formal document such as a **court-approved plan, final distribution statement, claim resolution, or final closure of the proceeding**. Recent KIS analogies point the same way: a court-plan payment for lost exchange assets was treated as **compensation rather than crypto disposal**, and in a theft case KIS attached importance to a **formal order discontinuing the investigation**. Those analogies help prove the facts, but they still do **not** create a separate PIT-38 loss box. ([Podatki][2])

**3. Is depositing crypto to a custodial platform a “disposal”?**

On a conservative reading, a bare transfer from self-custody to a **custody/wallet** account is **not** itself a disposal, because nothing listed in **art. 17 ust. 1f** has happened. The hard case is **Celsius Earn / BlockFi Interest Account**-type products: because Polish law treats exchange for a **“prawo majątkowe inne niż waluta wirtualna”** as disposal, a product that transferred title to the platform and left you only with a claim against it could arguably have been a disposal **on the deposit date**. For that question, the decisive evidence is the **product agreement** (Wallet/Custody vs Earn/BIA), not the blockchain transfer alone. ([Podatki][2])

If the account was in the lending/earn bucket and the deposit did amount to disposal, the tax moment would be the **deposit date**, not the later bankruptcy. Because your deposits were made before Polish residence, that would generally make it a **pre-Poland issue** rather than a current Polish PIT-38 event. This is the point most worth putting into an **individual ruling** if the amounts are material. ([Podatki][2])

**4. If the relevant event was in 2022 while you were Swedish resident, can it be claimed in Poland?**

As a separate **Polish 2022 crypto loss**: generally **no**. But KIS has confirmed that **documented crypto acquisition costs incurred before a move to Poland** can still be shown in PIT-38 when later **Polish-taxable crypto revenue** appears, provided those costs were **not already deducted abroad**. So pre-Poland Celsius/BlockFi coins may still matter in Poland through the **cost pool**, not through a stand-alone bankruptcy-loss deduction. ([Inforlex][3])

**5–7. FIFO and practical tracker treatment**

For Polish PIT, your tracker should **not** invent a zero-value sale on the bankruptcy date. The current PIT-38 design is **annual and aggregated**: crypto part E uses **poz. 36 revenue, 37 current-year costs, 38 prior unused costs, 39 income, 40 unused costs carried forward**, and KIS has stated that under this method you do **not** have to identify specific expenditures with specific revenues. At the same time, the statute now contains a **FIFO fallback** when exact identification is impossible. So Polish law is **not** a pure wallet-inventory system, but FIFO remains a defensible fallback rule inside a tracker. ([Podatki][4])

Direct answer to your practical options: choose **(c) something else**. The best Polish-tax posture is to move Celsius/BlockFi coins into a separate **“insolvency claim / disputed assets”** bucket. That means: **remove them from the spendable queue**, so they are not accidentally reused in later disposals, but **do not book them as sold for zero**. This avoids inventing a disposal the statute does not recognize while keeping the acquisition history attached to the claim. ([Podatki][2])

If **nothing is ever recovered**, keep the position as a closed or unresolved **claim record**, not as active inventory and not as a taxable zero-value disposal. If **crypto is partially recovered**, create a **recovered-asset sub-position** linked to the claim. Later sale of that recovered crypto is an ordinary crypto disposal, and KIS has emphasized that receipt of compensation in crypto and later sale of that crypto are **two separate events**. ([Inforlex][5])

If **cash is partially recovered**, book it as a **recovery/compensation event**, not as a sale of the old BTC. A recent KIS ruling treated a court-plan cash payout for lost exchange assets as **“other sources” compensation**, potentially exempt under **art. 21 ust. 1 pkt 3b** when it compensated actual loss under a court-approved plan; whether a Celsius or BlockFi distribution fits that model depends on the exact U.S. plan documents. The interaction between such a recovery and the original crypto cost pool is **not clearly settled** in a Celsius/BlockFi fact pattern, so I would not hard-code a netting rule without reviewing the actual distribution paperwork. ([Inforlex][6])

**8. Documentation**

Keep a full packet: original purchase records and bank transfers; on-chain txids to the Celsius/BlockFi deposit addresses you listed; platform balance screenshots/CSV/PDF before the freeze; product T&Cs showing whether the account was custody or lending; proof of claim; court plan/orders; distribution notices; and proof of every recovery received. KIS has accepted exchange reports plus bank confirmations as tax evidence, and Ordynacja says that anything lawful that helps clarify the facts may serve as evidence. The stronger the formal insolvency document, the easier it is to prove final partial non-recovery. ([Inforlex][7])

**9. Is there a specific PIT-38 field for platform-bankruptcy losses?**

No. There is **no dedicated PIT-38 field** for “platform bankruptcy loss.” On the current form for **2025 returns**, crypto is handled through **part E**: **poz. 36** revenue, **37** current-year crypto costs, **38** prior unused costs, **39** income, **40** unused costs carried forward; tax is then computed in **part F**. Part E is used not only when there is crypto revenue, but also when there are current-year or prior-year unused crypto costs. So any Celsius/BlockFi effect is reported, if at all, only through the **ordinary crypto revenue/cost boxes**, not through a special insolvency line. ([Podatki][4])

**Practical Polish filing posture**

Based on current Polish rules, the filing posture I would use is: **no stand-alone loss on the bankruptcy filing itself; preserve the original acquisition costs and any carryforward logic; move insolvent coins to a separate non-spendable claim bucket in the tracker; and classify any actual recovery separately as cash-compensation or recovered-crypto before finalizing the return for that year**. For a material amount, the two strongest individual-ruling questions are **(1)** whether your specific Celsius/BlockFi product was already an exchange for a non-crypto property right, and **(2)** how the actual distribution documents should be classified in Poland. ([Podatki][2])
