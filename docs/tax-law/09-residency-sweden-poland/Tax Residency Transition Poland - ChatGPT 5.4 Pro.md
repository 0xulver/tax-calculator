Below is the clearest rule set I can support from Polish and Swedish official materials. I am focusing on direct crypto acquisition/disposal; staking, lending, liquidity-pool, and other DeFi flows can create separate classification issues.

## Core rules

1. **Polish tax residence**: under art. 3 ust. 1a PIT, a person is Polish resident if they either **(a)** have their centre of personal or economic interests in Poland, **or (b)** spend **more than 183 days** in Poland in the tax year. Neither test has priority; the Ministry of Finance’s official explanation says the word **“lub”** means either test is enough, and each is assessed independently. Polish residents are taxed on **worldwide income**; non-residents only on **Polish-source income**. Poland’s treaty rules override the domestic residence rules via art. 4a PIT. ([ISAP][1])

2. **A move into Poland can happen mid-year**: the Ministry of Finance’s official residence guidance expressly says a change of tax residence can occur **during the tax year**. The current official “ulga na powrót” page also treats the period **from 1 January until the day before moving residence to Poland** as a pre-Poland period. So if the taxpayer genuinely moved in March 2023, the better official reading is that Polish residence starts when the residence actually shifted to Poland, usually the move date / centre-of-vital-interests shift, not automatically on 1 January. ([Podatki][2])

3. **Dual residence in 2023 is possible**: Sweden can continue to treat a departing person as tax resident if they retain **close ties** to Sweden, and Poland may simultaneously treat them as resident under art. 3 PIT. In that overlap, the treaty tie-breaker in article 4 applies in this order: **permanent home → centre of vital interests → habitual abode → nationality → mutual agreement**. ([Swedish Tax Agency][3])

4. **For direct crypto gains, the treaty’s best fit is article 13**: the Poland–Sweden treaty does not mention crypto expressly, but article 13(4) covers gains from the alienation of **“any property”** not dealt with in the earlier paragraphs, and that is the best fit for direct crypto gains. The treaty also has an unusual former-residence rule in article 13(5) allowing the former state to tax gains on **“any property”** for ten years after emigration. ([ISAP][4])

5. **But Sweden’s domestic post-exit rule appears narrower than the treaty**: Sweden’s domestic 10-year rule for former residents is limited to **delägarrätter** and certain partnership interests, while Skatteverket classifies bitcoin and similar crypto as **“andra tillgångar”** rather than equity-like assets. On that basis, direct crypto disposals after emigration do **not appear** to be covered by Sweden’s domestic 10-year rule, even though article 13(5) of the treaty is worded broadly enough to permit former-state taxation. That means post-move direct crypto sales are likely, in practice, a **Poland-only** capital-gains case once treaty residence is in Poland. ([Riksdagen][5])

6. **Poland does not appear to have an immigration step-up for crypto**: the Polish crypto rules use **documented direct acquisition expenditures** and sale-related costs, with foreign-currency amounts translated into PLN at the NBP average rate from the **last business day before the cost date** (for costs) and before the **income date** (for proceeds). I did not find a Polish PIT rule that rebases crypto to market value on the date Polish residence begins. ([ISAP][1])

7. **Swedish realized crypto losses do not transfer into Poland**: Poland carries forward only its own unused crypto **costs** under art. 22 ust. 14–16 / PIT-38, not foreign K4 losses. Historical cost of **unsold holdings** can matter in Poland later, but a **realized Swedish capital loss** does not become a Polish PIT-38 carryforward. ([Swedish Tax Agency][6])

## Answers to your numbered questions

1. **What makes someone a Polish tax resident? Which test takes precedence?**
   Either the **centre of vital interests** test or the **>183 days** test is enough. There is no statutory priority between them; the Ministry of Finance says each criterion is assessed independently. Days are counted by physical presence, including arrival/departure days and even part-days. ([ISAP][1])

2. **Could the taxpayer have been resident in both Sweden and Poland for part of 2023?**
   Yes. That is possible under domestic law if Poland regarded them as resident after the move while Sweden still treated them as resident because of close ties. In that case, the treaty tie-breaker decides the single **treaty residence** for the overlap period. ([Swedish Tax Agency][3])

3. **How does the Poland–Sweden DTT resolve dual residence?**
   Article 4(2) uses the standard sequence: **permanent home**, then **centre of vital interests**, then **habitual abode**, then **nationality**, then **mutual agreement** between the competent authorities. ([ISAP][4])

4. **If the move was in March 2023, is the taxpayer Polish resident for all of 2023 or only from March?**
   On the official Polish reading, usually **from the date the residence actually moved to Poland**, not automatically for the whole year. The MF guidance expressly allows an in-year residence change, and the official “ulga na powrót” page treats the period from the start of the year to the day before the move as a pre-Poland period. The caveat is factual: if the centre of vital interests had already shifted to Poland earlier than the physical move, the start date could be earlier. ([Podatki][2])

5. **Does Poland’s worldwide taxation mean all crypto trades from January 2023 onward are taxable in Poland, even before the move?**
   No, not automatically. Poland taxes worldwide income while the person is a **Polish resident**; before that, only Polish-source income is in scope. For a genuine March move, the pre-move Jan–Mar foreign crypto activity is not automatically pulled into Poland just because the person later became resident in the same year. Also, Poland does **not** tax crypto-to-crypto swaps, while Sweden does, so “all crypto trades” is too broad even after the move. ([ISAP][1])

6. **If Swedish resident Jan–Mar and Polish resident Apr–Dec, which country taxes Jan–Mar gains?**
   As a rule, the **Jan–Mar gains** belong to **Sweden** if the taxpayer was still Swedish resident then, and Poland generally should not tax those pre-move foreign gains. The key is the date of the taxable event, not just the calendar year label. ([Podatki][2])

7. **Does the DTT cover crypto gains, and which article applies?**
   Yes, the treaty can still apply even though crypto is not named. The best fit is **article 13(4)** as gains from alienation of “any property” not otherwise listed. For former Swedish residents, article 13(5) is relevant in theory, but for **direct crypto** Sweden’s domestic post-exit rule appears not to reach it. ([ISAP][4])

8. **Example: BTC bought in 2021 for EUR 30,000 in Sweden, sold in 2024 in Poland for EUR 50,000. What is the Polish basis?**
   For Polish PIT-38, the basis is the **historical documented acquisition cost**, not a migration-date value. In your example, the cost is **EUR 30,000 plus eligible fees**, converted into PLN at the NBP average rate from the **last business day before the 2021 purchase date**; the 2024 sale proceeds are converted into PLN at the rate from the **last business day before the 2024 sale date**. The taxable Polish crypto income is PLN proceeds minus PLN costs. ([ISAP][1])

9. **Is there any special step-up / step-down for assets acquired before becoming Polish resident?**
   I did **not** find a crypto-specific Polish PIT rule that rebases pre-immigration holdings to market value on the day Polish residence begins. The operative Polish rules continue to point to **documented acquisition expenditures** and carried-forward unused costs, not to an immigration-date fair market value. ([ISAP][1])

10. **If Swedish tax was already paid on 2020–2022 crypto gains, does Poland have any claim on those same gains?**
    Generally no, if the taxpayer was not Polish resident when those gains were realized. Poland taxes residents on worldwide income and non-residents only on Polish-source income; it does not reopen properly realized pre-Poland Swedish gains just because the person later moved to Poland. What Poland can tax later is a **new disposal** of a still-unsold asset while the taxpayer is Polish resident. ([ISAP][1])

11. **If the same transition-year gain were taxable in both countries, can Poland give relief for Swedish tax?**
    There are two layers. First, Polish domestic law for crypto contains a **foreign tax credit** rule in art. 30b ust. 5e–5f. But second, the treaty can override domestic law. Under the Poland–Sweden treaty, the Polish relief article is **article 22**, and for Polish residents it generally gives **exemption** for income that Sweden may tax, with **credit** reserved there for articles 10 and 12. So for Swedish-taxable capital gains under article 13, the treaty points more naturally to **exemption in Poland**, not an ordinary Polish credit. In practice, for direct post-emigration crypto gains there often is no Swedish tax to relieve at all, because Sweden’s domestic post-exit rule appears not to cover direct crypto. ([ISAP][1])

12. **Must PIT/ZG be filed for the transition year?**
    Not automatically. The official PIT-38 brochure says PIT/ZG is attached when foreign art. 30b income is **taxed in Poland using the credit method**. So for Sweden-transition-year crypto, PIT/ZG is generally relevant only if there is a genuine foreign-income / foreign-tax-credit case on the Polish PIT-38. If the gain belongs only to Sweden pre-move, or only to Poland post-move, or treaty relief is exemption rather than credit, PIT/ZG on the crypto side is generally not the right annex. ([podatki][7])

13. **For the calculator: use original purchase price or market value on becoming Polish resident?**
    Use the **remaining historical cost**, not migration-date market value. For the **Swedish-resident period**, crypto was taxed under Sweden’s **average-cost method** per coin type, not FIFO. For the **Polish-resident period**, I would preserve the remaining historical acquisition cost of the holdings that still existed when Polish residence began and convert foreign-currency costs to PLN under art. 11a. On the Polish PIT-38 itself you then report **annual totals** of proceeds, current-year costs, and prior-year unutilized costs; the return is not an immigration-date mark-to-market exercise. ([Swedish Tax Agency][6])

14. **Can Swedish K4 losses from 2022 be used in Poland?**
    No, not as Polish PIT-38 carryforwards. Sweden lets private individuals deduct only **70% of a crypto capital loss** there, under Swedish rules, but Poland’s crypto carryforward is limited to its own unused **costs** under art. 22 ust. 14–16. A Swedish realized loss stays Swedish. ([Swedish Tax Agency][6])

15. **What if the crypto came from a Swedish employer?**
    Then there are **two separate tax layers**. The receipt is first an **employment-income / benefit-in-kind** issue, not a PIT-38 capital-gains issue. Sweden’s official guidance says that if you receive crypto as salary, it is taxed as employment income when received, and when you later dispose of that crypto the basis is the **market value at receipt**—the same amount taxed as salary. Poland likewise treats employment income as money and benefits in kind under the employment-income rules, not PIT-38. So employer-provided crypto mainly creates a documentation problem: you need the payroll/tax value at receipt, because that is the key evidence for later basis tracking. If the work was performed after the move, the salary leg follows **article 15 (employment)** of the treaty, not article 13. ([Swedish Tax Agency][6])

## One important asymmetry for 2023

Sweden taxes **selling, swapping, and paying with crypto** as taxable disposals and uses an **average-cost** method in SEK. Poland, by contrast, expressly says that **crypto-to-crypto exchanges are not taxed**; Poland taxes only exchanges into fiat, goods/services, other property rights, or settlement of liabilities. So for an active trader, a 2023 event can be taxable in Sweden and not taxable in Poland even before you get to treaty questions. ([Swedish Tax Agency][6])

## Practical takeaway for this taxpayer

For a March 2023 move, the working model should usually be: **Sweden** taxes pre-move Swedish-resident disposals; **Poland** taxes post-move worldwide crypto disposals while Polish resident; there is **no identified Polish immigration step-up**; pre-Poland holdings keep their **historical cost**, translated into PLN under art. 11a; and **Swedish realized losses do not transfer** into Poland. The hardest cases are holdings that were reshaped by Swedish pre-move swap chains, lending/LP transfers, staking, or employee awards, because those require reconstructing the historical basis that actually remained on the day Polish residence began. ([Podatki][2])

One compliance point: Poland’s official guidance says **PIT-38 must be filed not only when you sell crypto, but also when you incur deductible crypto acquisition costs**, and unused costs carry forward. So the missing **2023** and **2024** Polish returns should be reviewed against the exact Polish-residence start date and the taxpayer’s post-move crypto activity. ([podatki][8])
