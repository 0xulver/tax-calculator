# Sweden → Poland Tax Residency Transition: Crypto Tax Analysis

## Executive Summary

This report addresses the full tax implications of a taxpayer who was a Swedish resident until mid-2023 and became a Polish tax resident, holding cryptocurrency acquired from 2020 onwards. The key conclusions are:

1. **Polish residency begins on the exact date the centre of vital interests shifted** — not retroactively from 1 January.
2. **The Sweden-Poland DTT Art. 13(4) assigns exclusive taxing rights on crypto capital gains to the country of residence at the time of disposal** — meaning Polish-period gains are taxed only in Poland.
3. **Sweden's Art. 13(5) 10-year look-back clause exists** in the DTT but its practical reach on crypto (not Swedish shares) is limited.
4. **Pre-Poland acquisition costs (2020–2022) ARE deductible in Poland's PIT-38**, based on a landmark 2024 Warsaw court judgment, using the original NBP rate from the purchase date — no step-up to market value at the date of becoming resident.
5. **Swedish crypto losses cannot be transferred to Poland** under any mechanism.
6. **Staking rewards carry material tax risk** in Poland — treated as property rights income under PIT-36, not PIT-38.

## Part 1: Determining Polish Tax Residency

### The Two-Criterion Test (Art. 3(1a) PIT Act)

A natural person is a Polish tax resident if they meet **either** of these conditions:[^1][^2]

1. **Centre of vital interests** (centrum interesów życiowych) in Poland — meaning the centre of personal interests (family location, permanent home, social ties) **or** the centre of economic interests (main income source, business, assets, bank accounts); **or**
2. **Physical presence** of more than 183 days in Poland in the tax year.

The conjunction "or" is legally significant: satisfying either one criterion is sufficient; both need not be met. The Ministry of Finance's 2021 Tax Explanations and jurisprudence of the Supreme Administrative Court (II FSK 2653/16) have established a **hierarchy**: the centre of vital interests is the **primary, qualitative criterion**, and the 183-day rule is a **subsidiary, quantitative fallback** applied only when the centre of interests cannot be unambiguously determined.[^2]

**What factors determine the centre of vital interests:**

| Category | Relevant Factors |
|---|---|
| Personal interests | Location of spouse/children, permanent dwelling, social/cultural ties, club memberships |
| Economic interests | Main source of income, business registration, assets and investments, bank accounts, location of asset management |

A taxpayer who relocates family, closes Swedish rental, registers a Polish address, opens Polish bank accounts, and begins operating their business from Poland will typically have their centre of vital interests in Poland from the date of that relocation — even if they have not yet crossed 183 days in the calendar year.[^1][^2]

### When Does Polish Residency Begin?

Polish residency operates **prospectively, not retroactively**. A KIS (Director of National Tax Information) interpretation dated 4 February 2025 (ref. 0114-KDIP3-2.4011.944.2024.2.JM) confirmed: *"From the day of arrival in Poland, the taxpayer becomes a Polish tax resident."*[^2]

If the taxpayer moved in, say, March 2023, their Polish residency begins on the specific date in March when the centre of vital interests transferred — not from 1 January 2023. Polish unlimited tax liability (worldwide income) applies only from that date forward.[^3][^2]

## Part 2: Dual Residency and the Transition Year (2023)

### Can There Be Dual Residency?

Yes — under domestic law, both Sweden and Poland may claim residency simultaneously for an overlapping period. Sweden will typically consider a person resident until they have sufficiently severed ties (deregistered from Folkbokföring, sold or given up permanent housing, moved family, ceased income connections). Poland will claim residency from the date the centre of vital interests shifts. There is a window where both countries may assert residency under their respective domestic rules.[^4]

### The DTT Tie-Breaker Rules (Art. 4, Sweden-Poland Convention)

When dual residency exists under both countries' domestic laws, the Sweden-Poland DTT Art. 4(2) resolves the conflict through sequential tie-breaker criteria:[^5]

1. **Permanent home** — The state where a permanent home is available. If in both states, proceed to step 2.
2. **Centre of vital interests** — The state with closer personal and economic ties.
3. **Habitual abode** — The state where the person usually stays.
4. **Nationality** — The state of citizenship.
5. **Mutual agreement** — Between the competent authorities of both states.

In practice, the taxpayer who moved their family, established a permanent apartment in Warsaw, and shifted their business operations to Poland will typically be assigned to Poland under the centre of vital interests criterion, regardless of any continuing formal links to Sweden.[^1][^2]

### Split Residency in Practice

**The transition year is split at the moment the centre of vital interests moved:**

| Period | Tax Status | Obligation |
|---|---|---|
| Jan – [move date] 2023 | Swedish resident | Unlimited liability in Sweden; limited (source-only) in Poland |
| [Move date] – Dec 2023 | Polish resident | Unlimited liability in Poland (worldwide income); limited (source-only) in Sweden |

Crypto gains realised **before the move date** in 2023 are taxed in Sweden. Crypto gains realised **after the move date** are taxed in Poland. The annual Polish PIT return for 2023 must reflect only the post-move period's worldwide income.[^3][^2]

## Part 3: The Sweden-Poland DTT and Crypto Capital Gains

### Which Article Applies to Crypto?

The Poland-Sweden Convention (signed Stockholm, 19 November 2004) does not mention virtual currencies or cryptocurrency specifically — it predates the asset class. Crypto gains fall under the treaty's **residual capital gains provision**.[^5]

**Article 13 of the DTT — Capital Gains, in summary:**

| Paragraph | Asset type | Taxing right |
|---|---|---|
| Art. 13(1) | Immovable property | State where property situated |
| Art. 13(2) | PE business property (movables) | State where PE located |
| Art. 13(3) | Ships/aircraft in international traffic | Residence state |
| **Art. 13(4)** | **All other property (residual — including crypto)** | **Exclusively the state of residence of alienator** |
| Art. 13(5) | Any property — where individual was formerly resident | Former state may tax for 10 years post-departure |

**Crypto qualifies as "any other property" under Art. 13(4).** Gains are therefore taxable **exclusively** in the Contracting State of which the alienator is a resident at the time of disposal. This means:[^5]

- Crypto sold while a **Polish resident** → taxable **only in Poland** under the DTT.
- Crypto sold while a **Swedish resident** → taxable **only in Sweden** under the DTT.

### The 10-Year Look-Back Rule (Art. 13(5))

Article 13(5) of the Poland-Sweden DTT creates an important carve-out:[^5]

> *Gains from the alienation of any property derived by an individual who has been a resident of a Contracting State and who has become a resident of the other Contracting State, may be taxed in the first-mentioned State if the alienation of the property occurs at any time during the **ten years** next following the date on which the individual ceased to be a resident of the first-mentioned State.*

**Critical limitation:** The same article specifies that the gain so taxed by Sweden **shall not include the gain, if any, that accrues during the period during which the individual is or was a resident of the other Contracting State** (i.e., Poland). In practice, Sweden could only tax the **Swedish-period accrued gain** — the appreciation from original cost basis up to the date of departure from Sweden. The Polish-period appreciation is explicitly excluded.[^5]

**Practical implications of Art. 13(5):**
- Sweden has a 10-year window (from the date Swedish residency ended) to assert taxing rights over crypto sold while Polish resident.
- Sweden's assertion is limited to the gain that accrued during Swedish residency (the "Swedish-period gain").
- Under Swedish domestic law, the 10-year extended liability rule (Chapter 3, Section 19 of the Swedish Income Tax Act) applies primarily to **Swedish shares and comparable securities** — its application to cryptocurrency (classified as "other assets" / *tillgång* by Skatteverket) is not settled and Skatteverket has not formally extended the 10-year rule to crypto.[^6][^4]
- Sweden has not implemented exit tax or deemed disposal rules for crypto upon departure.[^7][^6]

**In practice:** If the taxpayer holds BTC bought in Sweden in 2021 and sells it in 2024 while a Polish resident, Poland taxes the entire gain. Sweden *could* assert taxing rights on the Swedish-period portion under Art. 13(5), but: (a) the DTT limits this to pre-departure appreciation; (b) Sweden's domestic extended liability rules target Swedish shares, not crypto; (c) there is no known Swedish enforcement practice treating departure from Sweden as a taxable event for crypto. The risk exists, but Sweden's practical ability to enforce a claim on crypto (as opposed to Swedish listed shares) is limited.

### Elimination of Double Taxation Under the DTT (Art. 22)

Under Art. 22 of the Poland-Sweden DTT, the double taxation elimination methods differ depending on income type:[^5]

| Method | Applies to | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Exemption method** | Capital gains (Art. 13) and most other income | Poland exempts income that "may be taxed in Sweden" |
| **Credit method** | Dividends (Art. 10), Royalties (Art. 12) only | Poland allows deduction of Swedish tax paid |

**For crypto capital gains, the exemption method applies under Art. 22(1)(a).** If the DTT allocates taxing rights to Poland (Art. 13(4)), then Sweden should not tax at all. If Sweden taxes on any basis, Art. 22(2)(a) requires Sweden to credit Polish tax.

**Important:** Under Art. 22(1)(a), Poland applies the "exemption with progression" method — not a direct credit. For 19% flat-rate capital gains (PIT-38), this distinction is largely academic since the flat rate does not scale with income level.

## Part 4: Cost Basis for Pre-Poland Crypto

### The Landmark 2024 Court Ruling

The most important recent development for cross-border crypto holders is the judgment of the Voivodship Administrative Court in Warsaw of 29 August 2024 (ref. III SA/Wa 1290/24):[^8][^9][^10]

> **Both the tax authority and the court held that costs incurred in acquiring cryptocurrencies prior to obtaining residency in Poland could be included in the PIT-38 return, provided they had not been accounted for as tax expenses in another country.**

This ruling — issued in the context of a taxpayer who moved from the UK to Poland — establishes the following rule framework applicable equally to a Sweden-to-Poland transition:

| Situation | Deductible in Poland? |
|---|---|
| Crypto bought with cash/fiat while in Sweden (2020–2022) | **YES** — deductible as acquisition cost in PIT-38, if not deducted in Sweden |
| Crypto acquired via crypto-to-crypto swap while in Sweden | **NO** — court refused; no legal basis in PIT Act for recognising swap costs |
| Costs already deducted in Sweden (e.g., losses claimed on K4) | **NO** — cannot deduct the same cost twice |

### How to Convert Pre-Poland Costs to PLN

Polish law requires all foreign currency transactions to be converted to PLN at the **NBP average exchange rate published on the day preceding the transaction**:[^11][^12][^13]

- The NBP rate used is the rate from **the day before the original purchase date** — not the rate at the date of becoming a Polish resident.
- There is **no step-up in basis** to market value at the date of residency change. Poland does not implement a "deemed acquisition" or "cost rebasing" mechanism for incoming residents.
- Sweden likewise does not impose a deemed disposal on crypto when a taxpayer departs (no exit tax on crypto).[^6][^7]

**Practical example:**
- BTC purchased 15 March 2021 for EUR 30,000 (Swedish residency).
- NBP EUR/PLN average rate on 14 March 2021: assume 4.63.
- Polish cost basis: EUR 30,000 × 4.63 = **PLN 138,900**.
- BTC sold 10 June 2024 for EUR 50,000 (Polish residency).
- NBP EUR/PLN rate on 9 June 2024: assume 4.30.
- Polish proceeds: EUR 50,000 × 4.30 = **PLN 215,000**.
- Taxable income: PLN 215,000 – PLN 138,900 = **PLN 76,100**.
- Polish tax (19%): **PLN 14,459**.

The entire gain is taxed in Poland. Sweden has no withholding or collection mechanism on this transaction because: (a) the crypto is not Swedish listed shares; (b) the taxpayer is no longer Swedish-resident.

### The "No Double Deduction" Condition

The court's ruling includes a critical condition: pre-Poland costs are deductible in PIT-38 **only if they were not previously accounted for as tax-deductible costs in Sweden**. Operationally:[^9][^8]

- If the taxpayer filed Swedish K4 and declared a crypto **gain** in 2021, the cost basis was used in Sweden → that specific lot's acquisition cost cannot be re-used in Poland for PIT-38 (it has already been "consumed" in a Swedish disposal calculation).
- If the taxpayer held the crypto throughout and only reported purchases (never sold in Sweden), those costs are fresh and deductible in Poland.
- If the taxpayer declared a **loss** in Sweden (e.g., sold at a loss in 2022, claimed 70% deduction under K4), the cost basis was consumed in that Swedish loss calculation → not re-deductible in Poland.

### Aggregate Cost Method (Poland's Crypto Accounting)

Poland does not strictly apply FIFO to crypto. KAS uses an **aggregate method**: in each tax year, all crypto sale proceeds are summed and all crypto acquisition costs for that year are summed. Unused (excess) costs carry forward indefinitely to subsequent years:[^14][^15]

```
Year income from crypto = Total proceeds (PLN) − Acquisition costs for the year (PLN) − Carried-forward costs from prior years
```

**For pre-Poland lots:** The acquisition costs from 2020–2022 (when in Sweden) were not filed in any Polish PIT-38 (the taxpayer was not yet a Polish resident). Based on the 2024 WSA ruling, these costs can be entered in the first Polish PIT-38 filing (for 2023 or the first year with a taxable event). However, a KIS interpretation from June 2025 noted that costs must be declared in the year they were incurred; if they were not declared, a **correction (korekta)** of prior PIT-38 filings may be required. Since no Polish PIT-38 was filed for pre-2023, this tension is resolved by the WSA ruling, which allows historical pre-residency costs — but seek an individual tax ruling (interpretacja indywidualna) for certainty.[^16][^8][^9]

## Part 5: Swedish Tax Losses — No Transfer to Poland

Swedish crypto losses **cannot be carried forward to Poland or applied against Polish tax**:[^17][^18]

1. **Sweden's own rules:** Capital losses on crypto in Sweden can only offset gains in the **same Swedish tax year**. They cannot be carried forward to future Swedish years, let alone transferred to another jurisdiction.[^18]
2. **No treaty mechanism:** The Poland-Sweden DTT contains no provision for loss importation. Tax treaties eliminate double taxation on income; they do not create cross-border loss relief.
3. **Separate tax bases:** Polish PIT-38 crypto losses can only offset Polish crypto gains. Swedish K4 losses offset Swedish capital gains.

**Conclusion:** A crypto loss declared on a Swedish K4 for 2022 has zero tax value in Poland. It can only reduce Swedish taxes (for the same year or, under Swedish rules, reduce income tax via capital deficit rules).[^18]

## Part 6: Staking Rewards and DeFi Income

### Polish Treatment of Staking Rewards

The treatment of staking rewards in Poland is a **contested area** with material tax risk:[^19]

| Position | Form | Rate | When Taxed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **KAS / KIS (dominant view)** | PIT-36 (progressive scale) | 12%/32% or 19% linear | At time of receipt, at fair market value |
| **Taxpayer-favourable view** | PIT-38 (capital gains) | 19% | Only upon disposal of staked tokens |

The Director of National Tax Information (KIS) ruled on 19 April 2024 (ref. 0112-KDIL2-2.4011.146.2024.2.IM) that staking rewards constitute **income from property rights**, taxed as other income under the general scale (PIT-36), with tax arising at the time of receipt. This treatment creates a compounding problem: the staking reward is taxed on receipt (at progressive rates), and then if the token is later sold for fiat, the disposal is taxed again under PIT-38 at 19% — though the receipt-time value should count as the cost basis for the second event.[^19]

The cost basis on staking rewards received before Polish residency (Swedish period) presents the same pre-residency cost issue: the FMV at time of receipt (in Swedish period) would be the starting basis, but the court ruling only addressed fiat-purchased crypto costs. **Individual interpretation (interpretacja indywidualna) from KIS is strongly recommended** before including staking rewards in PIT-38.

### DeFi Activity (Liquidity Provision, Lending)

- **Crypto-to-crypto swaps** (including LP deposits/withdrawals in DeFi): **Tax-free in Poland** as a crypto disposal event under KAS guidance and the 2022 Supreme Administrative Court ruling (II FSK 1688/19). However, if the DeFi transaction is deemed a service exchange, the position may differ.[^20]
- **Interest/yield from lending protocols**: Analogous to staking — likely PIT-36 income on receipt.
- **Liquidations** on lending platforms (e.g., Hyperlend, Morpho): A collateral liquidation constitutes a crypto disposal and triggers capital gains calculation under PIT-38.[^21]

## Part 7: The Unfiled PIT-38 for 2023 and 2024

### Exposure Assessment

The taxpayer did not file PIT-38 for 2023 or 2024. The statute of limitations for Polish tax obligations is generally **5 years from the end of the calendar year in which the tax became due** (Art. 70 Tax Ordinance). For 2023, the clock runs until end of 2029; for 2024, until end of 2030.

**Obligation to file PIT-38 arises if:**
- Any crypto was sold for fiat (PLN, EUR, etc.) while a Polish resident[^22][^23]
- Any crypto was used to purchase goods or services[^22]
- Any crypto acquisition costs were incurred (even with no sales — to preserve the cost carry-forward)[^24][^13]

**Crypto-to-crypto transactions in Poland do not trigger PIT-38 reporting obligations** (unlike in Sweden, where every swap is taxable).[^25][^20]

### Recommended Corrective Steps

1. **Determine the exact date of Polish tax residency** in 2023 — supported by documentation (lease, registration, bank records, flight manifests).
2. **Reconstruct all crypto transactions** from the Polish residency start date through end-of-year — separating: (a) fiat disposals (taxable), (b) crypto-to-crypto (not taxable in Poland), (c) staking rewards received (potential PIT-36 exposure).
3. **Compile pre-Poland acquisition records** (2020–2022 purchases made with fiat in Sweden) — these form the deductible cost basis under the WSA 2024 ruling. Convert each purchase using the NBP EUR/PLN rate from the day preceding each purchase date.
4. **Exclude costs already reported on Swedish K4** — do not double-count any lots whose acquisition cost was used to compute a Swedish gain or loss.
5. **File corrective PIT-38 returns (korekta)** for 2023 and 2024 via e-PIT (Twój e-PIT) or submit original returns. Include PIT/ZG if any income earned from Swedish or other foreign sources after the move date.
6. **Obtain an individual interpretation (interpretacja indywidualna)** from KIS specifically on: (a) the exact date of residency commencement, (b) the inclusion of pre-residency acquisition costs using the original NBP rate, (c) treatment of staking rewards for the relevant years.

### PIT/ZG Filing Obligation

The PIT/ZG annex is required when foreign income is earned and a DTT double-taxation avoidance method (credit or exemption) must be applied. For the transition year 2023:[^26][^27]

- If the taxpayer had crypto sales or other income **while still Swedish resident** (before the move), those are Swedish income and do not appear in Polish PIT-38 (limited liability for that period).
- If, after becoming a Polish resident in 2023, the taxpayer received income from Swedish-source items (Swedish dividends, Swedish employment income, etc.), PIT/ZG would be required.
- Crypto sales conducted after becoming Polish resident are taxable in Poland only (Art. 13(4) DTT) — no PIT/ZG required for those, as the DTT assigns exclusive taxing to Poland and no Swedish tax is paid.

## Part 8: FIFO Calculator Rules for Pre-Poland Crypto Lots

### Cost Basis Rules Summary for the FIFO/Aggregate Calculator

| Parameter | Rule | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition cost currency** | Original EUR/USD/SEK/etc. price → convert to PLN | [^13] |
| **NBP rate for conversion** | Average NBP rate from the day **preceding** the purchase date | [^13][^12] |
| **Date to use** | Original purchase date (e.g., 2021 purchase uses 2021 NBP rate) — NOT the residency change date | [^8] |
| **Step-up to market value at residency change** | **No** — Poland has no such mechanism | [^8] |
| **Eligibility of pre-Poland lots** | Deductible if purchased with fiat (cash) and NOT previously deducted in Sweden | [^8][^9] |
| **Crypto-to-crypto acquisition cost** | Not deductible in Poland regardless of when acquired | [^8] |
| **Accounting method** | Aggregate (all costs vs. all proceeds per year) — not pure FIFO for crypto | [^14] |
| **Carry-forward of unused costs** | Indefinitely until matched against proceeds | [^14][^15] |
| **Staking reward cost basis** | PLN 0 (or FMV at receipt if PIT-36 income was declared on receipt) | [^28] |
| **Airdrop/fork cost basis** | PLN 0 | [^28] |

### The Aggregate Method vs. FIFO

Poland's official crypto tax guidance uses an **aggregate model**, not FIFO:[^29][^14]

- You do not match specific lots to specific sales.
- In each tax year: sum all proceeds in PLN from crypto-to-fiat disposals; subtract all acquisition costs incurred in that year **plus** all undeducted costs carried forward from prior years.
- The "Koszty niepotrącone" (non-deducted costs) line in PIT-38 tracks the carry-forward.

For a FIFO calculator, the practical implication is: the pre-Poland lots (2020–2022) contribute their PLN cost to the pool of "historical costs" that reduce the taxable base upon fiat disposal in Poland, but the timing of which lot is "sold" does not change the net tax since all costs and proceeds in a year are netted globally.
