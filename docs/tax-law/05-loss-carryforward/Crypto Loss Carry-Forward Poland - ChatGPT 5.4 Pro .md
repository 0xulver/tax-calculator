**Bottom line**

For Polish PIT, crypto does **not** use the ordinary 5-year tax-loss carry-forward from **art. 9 ust. 3**. **Art. 9 ust. 3a pkt 2** expressly excludes losses from **odpłatne zbycie walut wirtualnych** from that regime. Crypto uses a separate mechanism in **art. 22 ust. 14–16** and **art. 30b ust. 6a**: if eligible crypto costs exceed crypto revenue, taxable income is **0**, and the excess becomes **crypto costs for the next year**. The Ministry’s guidance states this directly: for crypto, a formal “strata” does not arise; instead, unused costs are deducted in later years. ([ISAP][1])

One important caveat: all of this assumes your annual PLN figures were already recalculated under **Polish crypto tax rules**, not exchange P/L. In Poland, taxable “odpłatne zbycie” means conversion to fiat, goods/services, other property rights, or paying obligations with crypto; **crypto-to-crypto swaps are neutral**. Eligible costs are direct acquisition/disposal costs, not financing costs, mining hardware, or electricity. ([ISAP][2])

## 1. Basic loss rules

**1) Does art. 9 ust. 3 apply to crypto?**
The general rules in **art. 9 ust. 3** are exactly as you described for sources where a tax loss is allowed: either up to **50% of the loss in any one year** over 5 years, or a **one-time deduction up to 5,000,000 PLN** in one of the next 5 years, with the remainder spread under the 50% cap. But **those rules do not apply to crypto**, because **art. 9 ust. 3a pkt 2** excludes losses from odpłatne zbycie walut wirtualnych. For crypto, the relevant rule is **art. 22 ust. 16**: excess costs roll into the next year as costs. ([ISAP][2])

**2) Can crypto losses offset other capital gains like stocks or bonds?**
No. Formally, crypto does not generate a deductible “loss” under art. 9 at all; it generates **unused crypto costs**. Those unused costs stay inside the crypto bucket. **Art. 30b ust. 5d** says income from disposal of virtual currency is **not combined** with income taxed under **art. 30b ust. 1** (which is the PIT-38 bucket for securities, derivatives, etc.) or with income under **art. 27** or **art. 30c**. So crypto cannot offset stock/bond gains, and stock/bond losses cannot offset crypto. ([ISAP][2])

**3) Can crypto losses offset business income (JDG / ryczałt)?**
As a rule, no. **Art. 17 ust. 1 pkt 11** classifies disposal of virtual currency as capital income, and **art. 17 ust. 1g** says this remains true even if the transactions occur within a business, except for the regulated AML-type activity in **art. 2 ust. 1 pkt 12** of the AML Act. The official tax portal also says you file **PIT-38 even when crypto transactions are done in a business**, except for that AML-service exception. In addition, **art. 30b ust. 5d** says crypto income is not combined with income taxed under **art. 27** or **art. 30c**, so it does not offset ordinary JDG income. For ryczałt, the practical result is also separation, because ordinary crypto trading is still reported in **PIT-38**, not inside the ryczałt bucket. ([ISAP][2])

**4) What exactly appears on PIT-38 if 2025 is a crypto “loss” year?**
On the **2025 PIT-38**, crypto is reported in **Part E/F**. In the 2025 form: **poz. 36** = crypto revenue, **poz. 37** = current-year crypto costs, **poz. 38** = prior-year crypto costs carried forward, **poz. 39** = income, and **poz. 40** = costs not deducted in 2025, to be carried to the next PIT-38. The brochure says the amount in **poz. 40** is the amount that goes into the next year’s return. There is **no crypto “strata” box** in this section. ([Gov.pl][3])

If 2025 has **0 revenue** and **15,000 PLN** of eligible crypto costs, the practical result is: **poz. 36 = 0**, **poz. 37 = 15,000**, **poz. 39 = 0**, **poz. 40 = 15,000**. If 2025 has **50,000 revenue** and **65,000 total eligible costs**, then **poz. 39 = 0** and **poz. 40 = 15,000**. That is why, in crypto, the tax return shows **0 income plus unused costs**, not a formal loss. ([Podatki][4])

## 2. Cross-border issues: Sweden to Poland

**5) If losses were incurred while the taxpayer was a Swedish tax resident, can those losses be carried forward in Poland?**
A **Swedish tax loss as such** does **not** become a Polish **art. 9** loss carry-forward. When a person is not Polish tax resident, Poland generally taxes only Polish-source income; when the person becomes Polish resident, Poland taxes worldwide income. For crypto, the Polish mechanism is not imported foreign loss carry-forward but **recognition of eligible crypto costs** under Polish rules. The most useful published KIS guidance on change of residence says something narrower: **documented crypto costs incurred before the move, and not previously deducted, may be shown in PIT-38 after becoming Polish resident**. ([ISAP][2])

So for your **2020–2022 Sweden** years, the right question is not “Can Swedish losses be imported into Poland?” but rather: **Were there documented acquisition costs from those years that were never deducted in Sweden and can still be supported?** If yes, published KIS interpretations for Germany/UK support recognizing such costs in Poland after the move. If no, there is nothing to carry into Poland. ([Inforlex][5])

**6) If the taxpayer filed a Swedish return showing crypto losses, can the same losses also be claimed in Poland?**
That would be very difficult to defend. The published change-of-residence interpretations approve Polish recognition only where the pre-move crypto costs were **not deducted abroad**. The UK interpretation expressly relied on the fact that the costs were **not deducted in the UK**, and the German interpretation referred to costs that were **poniesione i nieodliczone** before the change of residence. So the best reading is: **no double counting**. In other words, if the same costs already reduced Swedish tax, using them again in Poland would be a second deduction. That conclusion is an inference from the condition built into the interpretations. ([Inforlex][6])

**7) If Polish residence started mid-2023, can losses from the second half of 2023 onward be carried forward?**
Yes, subject to one correction in wording: what carries forward for crypto is **unused costs**, not a tax loss. Under **art. 3**, once Polish tax residence begins, Poland taxes worldwide income. So costs incurred **after** the residence change in 2023 are clearly Polish PIT-38 items. For costs incurred **before** the residence change, the published KIS interpretations support first recognition in the PIT-38 for the year the taxpayer became Polish resident, provided the costs were documented and were not deducted abroad. If foreign tax was paid on foreign-source crypto income after Polish residence began, Poland generally uses a **foreign tax credit**, reported via PIT-38 and, where applicable, **PIT/ZG**. ([ISAP][2])

## 3. Practical application

**8) If the 2025 crypto “loss” is 15,000 PLN, how much can be deducted in 2026?**
Assuming the **15,000 PLN** is really the excess of **eligible art. 22 ust. 14 crypto costs** over 2025 crypto revenue, then in 2026 the taxpayer can use **the full 15,000 PLN** against 2026 crypto revenue. There is **no 50% cap** and **no 5,000,000 PLN one-year cap** for crypto, because those are art. 9 loss rules and crypto is outside that regime. ([ISAP][2])

Example: if the 2025 return ends with **poz. 40 = 15,000**, and in 2026 the taxpayer has **40,000 PLN** of crypto revenue and **0** new crypto costs, taxable crypto income is **25,000 PLN**. If 2026 revenue is only **10,000 PLN**, taxable income is **0**, and the remaining **5,000 PLN** continues forward. Published KIS guidance says the crypto cost-carry rules contain **no maximum settlement period**. ([Podatki][7])

**9) Must the carry-forward be declared in the year of the loss, or can it be claimed later?**
For Polish years, it should be declared in the **year the cost was incurred**. **Art. 30b ust. 6a** says crypto costs must be shown in the annual return **even if no crypto revenue arose in that year**, and the Ministry’s crypto guidance says the same. The brochure also contemplates Part E for taxpayers who have **prior-year undeducted crypto costs**, even if they have no current-year crypto revenue. So the formal carry-forward chain is built year by year through PIT-38. ([ISAP][2])

If an earlier Polish PIT-38 omitted those costs, the proper fix is a **korekta** of that year’s PIT-38. The current PIT-38 guidance confirms that a return should be corrected when it contains errors or doubtful data. For crypto, that normally means correcting the original year and then any later PIT-38s whose carried-cost field depends on it. ([Podatki][8])

**10) If a corrected prior-year PIT-38 reveals a loss that wasn’t originally declared, can it be carried forward?**
For **crypto**, replace “loss” with **unused crypto costs**. Yes: if a corrected Polish PIT-38 shows higher eligible crypto costs and therefore higher unused costs, those corrected amounts should feed forward into later PIT-38s. Because the form uses a chain of annual carried-cost fields, in practice that often means correcting **more than one year**. ([Podatki][8])

For a **foreign pre-residency year** with no Polish filing obligation, the published UK/Germany interpretations allow a different route: undeducted pre-move costs can be shown **for the first time** in the PIT-38 for the year in which the taxpayer became Polish resident, instead of correcting a Polish return that never existed. ([Inforlex][5])

## 4. Documentation and filing

**11) What documentation is needed?**
The statute says crypto tax costs are **“udokumentowane wydatki”** directly incurred for acquisition plus disposal-related costs. A 2025 KIS interpretation adds that the taxpayer bears the burden of proof and must be able to show the expense was actually incurred and properly documented; the facts in that case included **transaction dates, acquisition prices, and payment confirmations**. For foreign-currency amounts, the PIT-38 brochure says values are translated to PLN using the **NBP average rate from the last business day before the revenue/cost date**. ([ISAP][2])

In practice, that means you want a defensible file containing at least: exchange histories, trade confirmations, dates of purchase/sale, acquisition prices, fee records, and proof of payment/funding that links the expenditure to the crypto purchase. Transaction evidence is generally **kept for audit**, not filed as an attachment to PIT-38; where there is foreign-source crypto income/tax to report, the explicit attachment mentioned by the brochure is **PIT/ZG**. ([Inforlex][6])

**12) Must the original loss year’s PIT-38 be filed (or corrected) before the carry-forward can be used?**
For **Polish** years, yes — that is the cleanest and safest route, because the statute requires reporting crypto costs in the year they arise, and the next year’s carried-cost field depends on the prior year’s return. So if a Polish 2023, 2024, or 2025 PIT-38 is wrong, the normal approach is to **correct that year first**, then propagate the correction forward. ([ISAP][2])

For **foreign pre-residency** years, published individual interpretations allow a different result: where the taxpayer had no Polish PIT-38 filing duty, **documented and undeducted** pre-move crypto costs may be entered **for the first time** in the PIT-38 for the year the taxpayer became Polish resident. Those interpretations are individual, not universally binding, but they are the clearest published Polish guidance I found on this point. ([Inforlex][5])

On the **2025 PIT-38** that should mean **0 crypto income** and **15,000 PLN carried forward** in the crypto-cost field; that full amount can then offset **2026 Polish crypto revenue**, without any 50% cap. ([Podatki][7])

Your **2020–2022 Sweden** years should **not** be treated as Polish 5-year crypto losses.
