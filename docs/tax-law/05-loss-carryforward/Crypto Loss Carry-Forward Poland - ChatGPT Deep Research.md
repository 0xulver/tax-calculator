# Crypto loss carry-forward rules in

## Overview of the key legal conclusions

Polish PIT law treats profits from “odpłatne zbycie walut wirtualnych” (paid disposal of virtual currencies) under a **19% flat tax** and reports them in **PIT-38, Part E**. The taxable base is the annual difference between crypto disposal revenue and crypto-related deductible costs (as defined in the PIT Act). citeturn29view0turn23view2turn27view0

For crypto specifically, the “loss carry-forward” that many taxpayers know from other PIT categories **does not exist in the usual Art. 9 ust. 3 sense**. Art. 9 ust. 3 provides a 5-year loss carry-forward with either a 50%/year cap or a one-time deduction up to PLN 5,000,000 (with the remainder subject to the 50% cap), but **Art. 9 ust. 3a explicitly excludes losses from paid disposal of virtual currencies**. citeturn23view1

Instead of carrying forward a “strata” (tax loss), Polish rules for crypto create a **carry-forward of unused costs**: if annual crypto revenue is lower than crypto deductible costs, the taxpayer’s taxable income is treated as **0**, and the **excess of costs over revenue increases the next year’s crypto costs** (Art. 22 ust. 16). Official government guidance summarizes this as: “Przy odpłatnym zbyciu kryptowaluty nigdy nie wystąpi strata” (there is never a tax loss in crypto disposal). citeturn23view2turn27view0turn20view0

This unused-cost carry-forward is effectively ring-fenced: crypto income is not combined with other PIT categories, and therefore crypto carry-forward costs do not offset (i) securities gains, (ii) other capital gains outside the crypto line, or (iii) business income taxed under scale/flat tax/ryczałt. citeturn29view0turn20view0

## Legal framework for crypto under PIT

### What is taxed and when

Under the PIT Act, revenue from virtual currencies is classified as income from “kapitały pieniężne” and includes “przychody z odpłatnego zbycia waluty wirtualnej.” citeturn12view0

“Paid disposal” of a virtual currency is defined (Art. 17 ust. 1f) as exchange of virtual currency for:
- legal tender (fiat),
- goods/services,
- a property right other than virtual currency,
- or paying other obligations with crypto. citeturn12view0turn27view0turn24view0

Government guidance also emphasizes that **crypto-to-crypto exchanges are not taxable disposal events** (“Wymiana pomiędzy walutami wirtualnymi… nie podlega opodatkowaniu”). citeturn27view0

### How income is computed and why “income cannot be negative”

The core computational rule (statute + official guidance) is:

- **income = revenue − costs**  
- if revenue < costs, taxable income is treated as **0** (not a negative number). citeturn27view0turn29view0

That “floor at zero” is the reason the system produces **unused costs to carry forward** instead of an Art. 9 “loss.” citeturn27view0turn23view2

## Art. 9 loss carry-forward and why it does not apply to crypto

### The general Art. 9 ust. 3 rule

Art. 9 ust. 3 regulates how a “strata ze źródła przychodów” (tax loss from a given source of income) can be deducted in later years. It gives two methods: citeturn23view1

- **Five consecutive tax years**, with the deduction in any year capped at **50% of the loss**, or citeturn23view1  
- a **one-time deduction up to PLN 5,000,000** in one of those five years, with the remainder carried forward to the remaining years, still subject to the **50% per-year cap**. citeturn23view1

This is the rule your question refers to (Art. 9 ust. 3 pkt 1 vs pkt 2). citeturn23view1

### The crypto carve-out: Art. 9 ust. 3a pkt 2

Art. 9 ust. 3a then states that the Art. 9 ust. 3 loss rules **do not apply** to certain categories of losses, including explicitly: **“z odpłatnego zbycia walut wirtualnych”** (losses from paid disposal of virtual currencies). citeturn23view1

This answers directly which version applies to crypto:

- The 50%/year rule and the PLN 5,000,000 one-time rule are **irrelevant for crypto disposals**, because the underlying legal construct (“strata” carry-forward under Art. 9 ust. 3) is switched off for crypto. citeturn23view1turn27view0turn20view0

### The crypto replacement mechanism: Art. 22 ust. 14–16

Crypto instead uses a “cost carry-forward” mechanism:

- deductible crypto costs are **documented direct acquisition costs** plus **documented disposal-related costs** (fees/commissions) (Art. 22 ust. 14), citeturn23view2turn27view0turn24view0  
- costs are generally deducted in the year incurred (Art. 22 ust. 15), citeturn23view2  
- but **if costs exceed crypto disposal revenue**, the excess **increases next year’s crypto costs** (Art. 22 ust. 16). citeturn23view2turn27view0turn20view0

Government guidance summarizes this as: you do not “deduct a loss”; you carry unused costs into future years. citeturn27view0turn20view0

## What crypto can offset and what it cannot

### Crypto vs other PIT-38 capital gains (stocks, derivatives, funds)

PIT-38 contains (at least) two conceptually different buckets:

- Part C/D: securities/derivatives/funds etc. taxed under Art. 30b ust. 1 and eligible for “strata” rules, citeturn32view0turn23view1turn20view0  
- Part E/F: crypto taxed under Art. 30b ust. 1a with the cost-carry mechanism. citeturn29view0turn33view0turn24view0

The ring-fencing is explicit: **“Dochodów z odpłatnego zbycia walut wirtualnych nie łączy się z dochodami… [z Art. 30b ust. 1]”**. citeturn29view0

Practical impact:

- Unused crypto costs carried forward (Art. 22 ust. 16) reduce only future crypto taxable income, not securities income. citeturn29view0turn23view2turn33view0

### Crypto vs business income (JDG / ryczałt / scale / 19% flat)

The statute and official guidance point in the same direction:

- Crypto income is not combined with business income taxed under Art. 27 (scale) or Art. 30c (19% linear business tax). citeturn29view0  
- The official podatki.gov.pl guidance states you still file PIT-38 even if you buy/sell crypto “w ramach prowadzonej działalności gospodarczej” (within business activity), with a narrow exception for regulated activity referenced via the AML Act (entities providing certain virtual currency services). citeturn27view0turn12view0

So, for most “ordinary” JDG cases where the taxpayer is merely trading/investing, crypto remains reported in PIT-38 and does not offset business profits (including ryczałt). citeturn27view0turn29view0

## How crypto carry-forward is declared on PIT-38

### What appears on the PIT-38 form when costs exceed revenue

In the official PIT-38(18) form for tax year 2025 (published by entity["organization","Ministerstwo Finansów","finance ministry, poland"]), Part E is dedicated to crypto and uses **positions 36–40**. citeturn31view0turn33view0turn24view0

From the form layout (Part E): citeturn33view0

- **pos. 36** – crypto disposal **revenue** (Przychód)  
- **pos. 37** – crypto deductible costs **incurred in the tax year**  
- **pos. 38** – crypto deductible costs **from prior years not deducted in the previous year**  
- **pos. 39** – crypto **income** (Dochód) = pos. 36 − (pos. 37 + pos. 38)  
- **pos. 40** – **costs not deducted in the tax year** = (pos. 37 + pos. 38) − pos. 36  

The official brochure (“Broszura do PIT-38 za 2025 r.”) explains the same mechanics and states that pos. 40 (the undeducted cost) is the amount that will be entered in the next year’s PIT-38. citeturn24view0

### How the carry-forward chain works across years

The brochure specifies that in the next year:

- the **new year’s pos. 38** is essentially the amount carried forward from the prior year (it instructs taxpayers to populate it from the prior PIT-38), and  
- the prior year’s **pos. 40** is the “unused costs” amount that feeds into that carry-forward. citeturn24view0turn33view0

This is the formal mechanism by which a “2025 net loss” becomes a **2026 cost carry-forward**.

### Filing even with no revenue

Two independent sources require reporting costs even if there was no crypto revenue:

- Art. 30b ust. 6a: in the PIT-38 return, the taxpayer reports crypto costs “także wtedy, gdy… nie uzyskał przychodów” (also when no crypto disposal revenue was obtained). citeturn29view0  
- Official guidance: you file PIT-38 if you had paid acquisition (“odpłatne nabycie”) or paid disposal; and you must show the costs regardless of whether there was a sale in the same year. citeturn27view0turn20view0

This matters because, in practice, the carry-forward chain relies on the prior-year declared amounts. citeturn24view0turn33view0

## Cross-border years and the Sweden-to-Poland transition

### Residency determines whether Poland taxes those years at all

Polish domestic law distinguishes:

- **unlimited tax liability** for individuals with a place of residence in Poland (taxed on worldwide income), and  
- **limited tax liability** for non-residents (taxed only on Polish-source income). citeturn19view0turn24view2

A person is treated as resident in Poland if they (i) have their centre of personal/economic interests in Poland, or (ii) stay in Poland more than 183 days in the tax year. citeturn19view0turn24view2

The official residency explanation also notes that a change of tax residence can occur during a tax year and describes a split approach (resident status up to the change date vs limited liability after moving abroad). citeturn25view0

### Treaty tie-breaker for Sweden–Poland

If an individual is treated as resident of both states under local laws, the Poland–entity["country","Sweden","country in europe"] treaty provides tie-break rules based on (in order) permanent home, centre of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality, then mutual agreement. citeturn24view4turn24view5

This is the legal framework you need to apply to the 2020–2023 residency narrative before deciding whether any Polish PIT-38 should exist for those years. citeturn19view0turn24view4turn25view0

### Can Swedish-year crypto “losses” be carried into Poland?

Two separate issues must be separated:

**Swedish-year trading results (closed transactions).**  
If the taxpayer was not Polish tax resident and the transactions were not taxable in Poland (limited liability), those annual “net losses” are not “Polish PIT losses” and cannot be imported into Poland as a carry-forward. This is true even before you reach crypto-specific rules; it follows from the residence/obligation structure. citeturn19view0turn25view0turn24view4

**Cost basis for coins later disposed of while resident in Poland.**  
If the taxpayer bought crypto while abroad and later disposed of it during a year in which Poland taxes the disposal, Polish deductible costs are defined as documented direct acquisition costs and disposal costs—without a requirement that the acquisition occurred in Poland or during Polish residency. citeturn23view2turn29view0turn27view0

So, while you generally cannot “carry forward a Swedish crypto loss” into Poland as such, you may still need those historical acquisition records to support deductible costs in Poland when those coins are later disposed of. citeturn23view2turn27view0

### Double counting concern (Sweden return and Poland return)

Polish law does not provide a mechanism to “reuse” a foreign-year crypto trading loss as a Polish loss carry-forward (and in crypto, Art. 9 loss carry-forward is excluded anyway). citeturn23view1turn20view0turn27view0

Where the same disposal is taxed in both countries (possible in transition years), Polish law provides a foreign tax credit mechanism for crypto disposals (Art. 30b ust. 5e–5f) and PIT-38 includes a dedicated line for foreign tax paid, capped by a proportional limitation. citeturn29view0turn28view0turn33view0

Practically, that means transition-year coordination should generally be about:
- ensuring the *same disposal* is not taxed twice without relief, and  
- ensuring acquisition costs are not deducted twice for the *same* disposal event, because costs are meant to compute the income base for that disposal. The Polish system is set up to handle foreign tax paid (credit), not to import foreign “loss carryforwards.” citeturn29view0turn28view0turn23view2

## Practical examples aligned to your 2025 “loss” scenario

### Translating “2025 loss of 15,000 PLN” into Polish PIT-38 mechanics

Under Polish crypto rules, a “loss” year means:

- annual crypto revenue (pos. 36) is lower than total costs (pos. 37 + pos. 38),  
- taxable income (pos. 39) is **0**,  
- the difference becomes **pos. 40 (unused costs)** to carry forward. citeturn27view0turn33view0turn24view0

Assume (illustrative) that in 2025:
- pos. 36 (revenue) = 10,000 PLN  
- pos. 37 (costs in 2025) = 25,000 PLN  
- pos. 38 (prior-year unused costs) = 0 PLN  

Then:
- pos. 39 (income) = 0 PLN  
- pos. 40 (unused costs carry-forward) = 15,000 PLN citeturn33view0turn24view0turn23view2

That pos. 40 amount is the “carry-forward” you use in later years—not an Art. 9 “loss.” citeturn23view1turn20view0turn33view0

### How much can be deducted in 2026?

Your Question 8 has two alternatives (50% rule vs 5M rule). For crypto, neither applies; the controlling rule is Art. 22 ust. 16 (unused costs roll forward) plus the PIT-38 mechanics in Part E. citeturn23view2turn23view1turn33view0

So, with **15,000 PLN unused costs from 2025**:

- If 2026 crypto disposal revenue is **≥ 15,000 PLN** (and you have no other costs), you can effectively use the full 15,000 PLN in 2026 (by entering it in 2026 pos. 38 and letting it reduce 2026 taxable income). citeturn23view2turn20view0turn24view0  
- If 2026 crypto disposal revenue is **< 15,000 PLN**, taxable income is again 0 and the remaining unused costs carry further (again into the next year via the same pos. 40→pos. 38 chain). citeturn27view0turn24view0turn33view0

In other words, the system is not “15,000 spread across 5 years,” but “15,000 used as costs against future crypto revenue until exhausted.” citeturn23view2turn20view0turn27view0

### Year-by-year illustration for 2025–2027

| Tax year | Crypto revenue | Current-year costs | Prior-year unused costs brought in | Taxable crypto income | Unused costs carried out |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 2025 | 10,000 | 25,000 | 0 | 0 | 15,000 |
| 2026 | 20,000 | 2,000 | 15,000 | 3,000 | 0 |

This table is exactly what PIT-38 Part E is designed to express via positions 36–40. citeturn33view0turn24view0turn27view0

## Corrections, timing, and documentation

### Must the “loss year” PIT-38 be filed to use carry-forward later?

The safest “black-letter + form-design” answer is effectively **yes**:

- the statute requires reporting crypto costs even with no crypto disposal revenue (Art. 30b ust. 6a), citeturn29view0  
- the official guidance requires filing PIT-38 when crypto costs were incurred, even without revenue, citeturn27view0turn20view0  
- and the brochure shows that the carry-forward is implemented via values coming from the prior return’s positions. citeturn24view0turn33view0

If a cost year was not filed or was filed incorrectly, you normally resolve that with a correction (korekta). citeturn21view0turn31view0

### Can a corrected PIT-38 create (or increase) carry-forward?

Polish Tax Ordinance rules allow correcting a previously filed return by submitting a corrective return (Ordynacja podatkowa, Art. 81). citeturn21view0

If a correction increases crypto costs in an earlier year, it can increase that year’s unused-cost carry-forward (pos. 40), which then should flow into subsequent years’ “prior-year unused costs” field (pos. 38). Because the carry-forward is chained, it is common that correcting an earlier year implies you may also need to correct later years to keep the chain consistent with the brochure’s mechanism. citeturn24view0turn33view0turn21view0

### Time limits and how long to keep evidence

Polish tax liabilities generally become time-barred after **5 years from the end of the calendar year in which the tax payment deadline fell** (Ordynacja podatkowa, Art. 70 §1). citeturn22view0

Because crypto unused costs can carry forward and affect later years, the practical recordkeeping period often needs to cover at least the last year in which those costs were used, plus the limitation window for that year. The statute also includes multiple situations that can suspend the running of the limitation period. citeturn22view0turn23view2

### What documentation supports a crypto cost carry-forward

The statutory standard is “udokumentowane wydatki” (documented expenses) directly incurred to acquire virtual currency and documented selling-related costs. citeturn23view2turn27view0turn24view0

In practice, the documents that best support this (and help reconcile PLN values) usually include:

- exchange transaction histories (buys/sells/fees), wallet transfer records where relevant, and confirmations of commissions/fees as disposal costs; citeturn27view0turn23view2  
- bank transfer confirmations or other payment evidence that ties fiat outflows to crypto purchases (especially when the exchange report is incomplete); citeturn23view2turn24view0  
- records showing which expenses are **not** deductible (e.g., financing costs, crypto-to-crypto swap “costs,” mining hardware/electricity) because those categories are expressly excluded in official guidance; citeturn27view0turn20view0  
- internal calculation workpapers that bridge multiple exchanges and ensure you do not double-count costs already used. citeturn23view2turn33view0

Finally, government guidance notes that crypto intermediaries generally are not obliged to provide PIT-11/PIT-8C equivalents for crypto transactions, so the taxpayer must maintain and substantiate their own calculation file. citeturn27view0
