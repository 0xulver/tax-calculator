## Legal audit of assumptions

**Annual pooling rather than FIFO.** Correct. The statute makes crypto costs an annual figure that is deducted in the year incurred, with any excess carried forward to the next year. The official PIT-38 guidance tracks exactly that structure by separating current-year costs, prior-year unrelieved costs, and the carry-forward balance. This is not a FIFO regime. ## Implementation audit

I could not inspect the literal source lines, so this section audits the logic implied by your file map and behavior descriptions rather than the text of the code itself.

The most important design flaw is category mixing. Using one manual parser and one cost-ingestion path for exchange purchases, card purchases, and salary/compensation receipts collapses two different legal theories into one technical type. A purchase file is trying to prove a **direct expenditure** to acquire crypto. A salary file is trying to prove the **value of a compensation receipt**, which may or may not be recognized by Polish law as future PIT-38 basis. Those should not be normalized into the same legal bucket before classification.

The second design flaw is the deposit-cost shortcut. If a stablecoin deposit can become a PIT-38 cost event whenever that year is not already covered by a salary file, the engine is building costs from a transfer proxy rather than from acquisition evidence. That is not a filing-safe rule under article 22(14), and a year-level dedup check is not enough to protect against cross-year deposits, redeposits after self-custody, fragmented wallet inflows, or mixed balances sourced partly from purchases and partly from compensation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42search5Ņłéturn29search1Ņłü

The manual fee logic is also suspect. Adding a same-asset fee to PLN cost can be fine if the fee is a direct buy/sell fee. Adding that same fee **also** to the asset amount is only correct if the source file records a net credited amount and the code is intentionally reconstructing gross units. If that assumption is wrong, holdings will be overstated, and every later transfer or deposit heuristic becomes less reliable. For tax, a fee being deductible does not automatically mean more units were acquired. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn15search0Ņłéturn29search1Ņłü

On exchange normalizers, the real risk is not formatting but economic classification. Binance grouping is safe only if it groups by true order identity and preserves quote asset, fee asset, and side; otherwise it can accidentally turn non-taxable crypto-to-crypto legs into pseudo-buys or pseudo-sells. Kraken pairing is safe only if it joins actual ledger legs of one trade instead of nearest timestamps. FTX parsing must keep spot trades, fiat-exchange rows, and transfers distinct. Coinbase is the weakest path because there is no raw export to audit against the fallback manual file.

The ignored transaction classes need a sharper policy. Internal transfers and fiat deposits/withdrawals are usually fine to ignore for PIT-38. Staking rewards, earn allocations, airdrops, and similar rows generally do **not** create PIT-38 purchase cost because there is no direct acquisition expenditure, but they should still remain in the audit trail because otherwise later deposits can be misread as fresh cost events. Funding-fee rows should stay out of the crypto cost pool, but their presence may signal derivatives activity that belongs in a separate capital-gains review instead of being silently dropped. ## Corrections

The first correction is conceptual: split the PIT-38 opening pool into three legally distinct classes and stop filing them as one number. Class A should be documented direct purchase costs, including pre-residency exchange or card purchases that satisfy the 2025 KIS interpretation and were not deducted abroad. Class B should be post-residency service/barter receipts that were already taxed in Poland and therefore can feed future PIT-38 basis. Class C should be pre-residency salary/compensation paid in USDC. Class C should be **excluded by default** from the filing engine, or at minimum downgraded to an aggressive optional position rather than a built-in assumption. The effect of excluding class C is higher PIT-38 tax and lower carry-forward in every later year. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35search4Ņłéturn39search0Ņłéturn28search0Ņłü

The second correction is technical and legally important: delete the rule that lets stablecoin deposits create autonomous PIT-38 cost. Deposits should be reconciliation events only. Any cost should come from a linked acquisition record: exchange buy, card purchase, or Polish-taxed compensation receipt. Replace year-level dedup with evidence-level dedup keyed to transaction hash, invoice, contract payment reference, or exchange order identity. The effect of this change will usually be lower PIT-38 cost and lower carry-forward wherever the current logic is double-counting. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42search5Ņłéturn29search1Ņłü

The third correction is to the compensation path. For post-residency crypto remuneration, record the Polish-taxed receipt value **once** as ordinary income and **once** as future PIT-38 basis, using the value actually taxed under the contract/invoice logic. Do not then recreate that cost when the same units later hit an exchange. For pre-residency compensation, do not include the receipt value in PIT-38 without a consciously chosen aggressive position or an individual interpretation. The effect is to remove the repoŌĆÖs current overreach while preserving the defensible anti-double-tax treatment for post-residency barter receipts. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39search0Ņłéturn28search0Ņłéturn38search2Ņłü

The fourth correction is PIT-28. The health-insurance deduction must reduce revenue, not tax, and the revenue date must follow article 14 rather than a blanket invoice-date shortcut. On the figures you gave, the health-deduction fix alone raises 2025 PIT-28 by about 2,573 PLN. The revenue-date fix could move it up or down depending on the exact service periods, issue dates, payment dates, and historical EUR/PLN rates, but the current universal issue-date rule is not legally sufficient.
