## Bottom line

Yes. An overdue PIT-38 for 2023 or 2024 can still be filed. If no PIT-38 was filed at all, filing it now is a **late first-time declaration**, not a **korekta** of PIT-38. The safest compliance path is: prepare the overdue PIT-38(s), submit a **czynny żal** under **art. 16 Kodeksu karnego skarbowego**, and pay the tax plus late-payment interest immediately, or file an instalment request if full payment is not possible. e‑Urząd Skarbowy currently supports **Twój e‑PIT (including PIT‑38)**, **electronic czynny żal**, and **RAT‑ZW** instalment requests; annual PIT returns can also be filed through **e‑Deklaracje** or on paper. ([ISAP][1])

The core provisions here are: **KKS art. 16 and 16a** (czynny żal / correction safe harbor), **KKS art. 54** plus the definitions in **art. 53** and fine rules in **arts. 23, 47–48**, and **Ordynacja podatkowa arts. 53, 56, 63, 67a, 70, 81, 81b**. ([ISAP][2])

## 1) Late PIT-38: filing after the deadline

A **korekta** exists only for a declaration that was already filed. Ordynacja art. 81 says taxpayers may correct an **uprzednio złożona deklaracja**, and the correction is made by filing a correcting declaration. MF’s PIT‑38 guidance also uses “korekta” only for a PIT‑38 that already exists but contains errors. So if no PIT‑38 was filed for 2023 or 2024, filing now is a **first filing made late**, not a correction. If PIT‑36 or PIT‑37 was filed and is itself wrong, that return can be corrected separately. ([ISAP][1])

For crypto, PIT‑38 is the correct return. MF’s current PIT‑38 guidance says you must file PIT‑38 if you had **revenue from disposal of virtual currencies** or even just **costs** connected with such disposal, and MF explicitly treats exchange of crypto **into legal tender** as an **odpłatne zbycie waluty wirtualnej**. The PIT rate on virtual-currency disposal is **19%**. ([Podatki][3])

## 2) Is czynny żal required, and what is it?

For a **never-filed PIT‑38**, a czynny żal is not what makes the tax office “accept” the return; the return can still be filed late. But if the goal is to avoid **penal-fiscal punishment**, the protective mechanism is **art. 16 KKS**, not art. 16a. Art. 16a is for a **legally effective correction of an already-filed declaration** plus prompt payment. That is why, in your fact pattern, the safer route for the missing PIT‑38s is **czynny żal under art. 16 KKS**. ([ISAP][2])

Under **art. 16 §1 KKS**, the offender is **not punishable** if, after the act, they notify the authority and disclose the **material circumstances**, especially any cooperating persons. Under **art. 16 §2**, if there was a public-law underpayment, the amount must be paid in full within the term set by the authority. Under **art. 16 §4**, the notice may be written or oral to minutes, and under **art. 16 §4a** it may be filed through **e‑Urząd Skarbowy**. Under **art. 16 §5**, it is ineffective once the authority already has clearly documented knowledge of the offense or has started relevant official actions such as checking activities or control. ([ISAP][2])

So, to your question 7: an **effective** czynny żal does not merely reduce the penalty; it removes penal-fiscal punishment for the disclosed act. It does **not** erase the tax or the late-payment interest. ([ISAP][2])

## 3) What the czynny żal must contain

Art. 16 KKS is **content-based**. I did not find a mandatory statutory template in the official materials I checked. The essentials are: identify yourself and the competent office, state that you are notifying under **art. 16 KKS**, describe exactly what was omitted, give the relevant periods and forms, disclose the material circumstances and any cooperating persons if there were any, and state that the overdue returns are being filed and the tax/interest paid. KAS guidance also commonly expects identifying data and a short description of the event. ([ISAP][4])

A workable Polish draft:

```text
[Miejscowość], [data]

Naczelnik [właściwy Urząd Skarbowy]
[adres urzędu]

Zawiadamiający:
[Imię i nazwisko]
```

Use a **short, truthful, factual** explanation. Do not embellish, but do not omit material facts or cooperating persons if there were any. ([ISAP][2])

## 4) Can this be done electronically?

## 5) Tax due, interest, and how to pay

Annual PIT from the return is due by **30 April** of the following year. If it was not paid then, it becomes a tax arrears balance and **late-payment interest** runs from the **day after** the deadline. So for the 2023 PIT‑38, interest runs from **1 May 2024** to the payment date; for the 2024 PIT‑38, from **1 May 2025** to the payment date. The taxpayer self-calculates the interest. The amount is rounded to full złoty, and MF says no interest is collected if the total does not exceed **8.70 PLN**. ([Podatki][6])

As of **19 March 2026**, the currently published **basic** late-payment interest rate is **10.50% per year**, announced on **10 March 2026**. But for a 2023 or 2024 arrears calculation, do **not** just apply 10.50% to the whole period. Use MF’s calculator or professional software, because the exact interest must be calculated over the actual delay period using the rate(s) in force during the relevant subperiods. ([Monitor Polski][7])

You do not wait for a demand from the office: late interest is paid **without prior summons**. Both the PIT itself and PIT late-interest amounts are paid to your **mikrorachunek podatkowy**. For an annual settlement transfer, if you fill in the mikrorachunek, form type, year and amount, the transfer title can be left blank. ([ISAP][8])

For your question 10: if the 2023 PIT‑38 shows about **27,000 PLN** tax due, pay that amount **plus** late interest as soon as you file the overdue return. In practice, do it the **same day** as the filing and the czynny żal. ([ISAP][8])

## 6) Instalments (raty)

Yes. Under **Ordynacja art. 67a §1**, the tax authority may, on the taxpayer’s request and for important taxpayer/public-interest reasons, **postpone** payment or **split tax arrears and interest into instalments**. e‑Urząd Skarbowy currently lists **RAT‑ZW** for this. MF also notes that instalment relief can involve a separate **opłata prolongacyjna**. ([ISAP][1])

One caution: the no-punishment rule in **art. 16 §2 KKS** is tied to payment of the public-law amount within the authority’s term. So the instalment route is legally more delicate than simply paying everything immediately. If you need instalments for the 2023 arrears, involving a **doradca podatkowy** is prudent. ([ISAP][2])

## 7) Penalty exposure

For a completely missing PIT‑38 with tax due, the most obvious penal-fiscal provision is **KKS art. 54**: failure to disclose the subject/base or failure to file a declaration, thereby exposing tax to underpayment. If the tax exposed does **not** exceed the **ustawowy próg** (5 × minimum wage at the time of the act), it is a **tax misdemeanor** punished by a kwotowa fine. If it exceeds that threshold but is still **“małej wartości”** (not more than 200 × minimum wage), it is a **tax crime** punished by a fine of up to **720 daily rates**. ([ISAP][2])

For tax misdemeanors, the fine range is **1/10 to 20 × minimum wage**; with the **2026** minimum wage of **4,806 PLN**, that is **480.60 PLN to 96,120 PLN**, and a mandate can be up to **24,030 PLN**. For tax crimes, the court sets **10 to 720 daily rates**, and one daily rate ranges from **1/30 to 400 × minimum wage**, depending on the offender’s income and circumstances. ([ISAP][2])

Applied to your approximate facts: for the **2023 PIT‑38 omission**, the relevant **2024** ustawowy próg was **21,210 PLN** (5 × 4,242 PLN). A 2023 PIT exposure around **27,000–27,740 PLN** is above that threshold, so on the face of art. 54 it is **above the misdemeanor level**, though still well below **“mała wartość”** (**848,400 PLN**). For the **2024 omission**, the relevant **2025** ustawowy próg was **23,330 PLN** (5 × 4,666 PLN); a tax exposure around **570 PLN** on 3,000 PLN of taxable crypto income is far below that threshold and looks like a **tax misdemeanor**, unless the newly found 2024 sell-to-fiat events materially increase the unpaid tax. ([ISAP][4])

So the real risk year is **2023**. Without an effective czynny żal, 2023 is the year with the more serious penal-fiscal exposure. ([ISAP][2])

## 8) Limitation periods

For the **tax itself**, the ordinary rule is **5 years from the end of the calendar year in which the payment deadline expired**. That means, ordinarily, the 2023 PIT‑38 remains open until **31 December 2029**, and the 2024 PIT‑38 until **31 December 2030**. This is **not** counted from the end of the tax year. ([ISAP][8])

For **penal-fiscal liability**, the limitation rules are separate. Tax crimes generally lapse after **5 years** (or **10 years** for more serious crimes), with special rules for tax-exposure offenses. Tax misdemeanors lapse after **1 year**, extended to **2 years** if proceedings are started within that first year. ([ISAP][2])

## 9) Corrections to PIT-36 / PIT-37 and PIT-38

If PIT‑36 or PIT‑37 was filed and is otherwise correct, the absence of PIT‑38 does **not** turn PIT‑38 into a correction of PIT‑36/PIT‑37. PIT‑38 is its own separate return. In practice, that means: file the missing PIT‑38 now; correct PIT‑36/PIT‑37 only if those returns themselves contain errors. ([Podatki][9])

If corrections are needed to both for the same year, you simply file **separate documents**: a **late PIT‑38** for the missing crypto return, and a **korekta** of PIT‑36 or PIT‑37 if that return was wrong. Where a return was already filed and merely needs fixing, **art. 16a KKS** is the relevant no-punishment rule for a legally effective correction plus prompt payment. ([ISAP][8])

I do **not** see a numeric statutory cap on how many corrections may be filed. Practically, you can file further corrections until the liability is time-barred, subject to the correction right being suspended during a pending tax control or tax proceeding in the relevant scope. ([ISAP][8])

## 11) Risk assessment: with vs without czynny żal

With an **effective** czynny żal filed before the office has documented knowledge or started relevant actions, the statute says there is **no penal-fiscal punishment** for the disclosed act. The tax and the interest still remain fully payable. ([ISAP][2])

Without a czynny żal, voluntary late filing still helps factually because you are coming forward, but it does **not** give you the art. 16 no-punishment shield. The office can still pursue the tax, interest, and penal-fiscal fine exposure, with the 2023 year being the more significant one on your numbers. ([ISAP][2])

If the office contacts you first, starts checking actions, or already has clearly documented knowledge, **art. 16 §5** can make the czynny żal ineffective. That is why prompt action materially improves your position. ([ISAP][2])
