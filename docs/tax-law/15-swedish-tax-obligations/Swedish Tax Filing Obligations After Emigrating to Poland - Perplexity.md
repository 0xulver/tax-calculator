# Swedish Tax Filing Obligations After Emigrating to Poland

## Executive Summary

**Bottom line:** This taxpayer is almost certainly *begränsat skattskyldig* (limited tax liability) in Sweden. None of their income — USDC service payments, JDG freelance EUR, or crypto trading — constitutes Swedish-source income. The Sweden–Poland Double Tax Treaty (DTT, 2004) assigns all taxing rights to Poland across every income category. The Swedish 10-year rule (*tioårsregeln*, 3 kap. 19 § IL) does not apply to cryptocurrency. **The legally sound position is: no Swedish tax return is required for 2023, 2024, or 2025.** The most urgent practical step is to formally deregister from the Swedish Population Register (*Folkbokföringen*) if not yet done, and to obtain a Polish certificate of tax residence as documentary backup.

***

## 1. Swedish Tax Status: Begränsat or Obegränsat Skattskyldig?

Under *Inkomstskattelagen* (IL) 3 kap. 3–8 §§, a natural person is subject to *obegränsad skattskyldighet* (unlimited tax liability) in Sweden if they meet **any one** of three grounds:[^1][^2]

1. **Domicile (*bosatt*)** in Sweden — actual place of residence registered in Sweden
2. **Habitual stay (*stadigvarande vistelse*)** in Sweden — typically ~6 consecutive months or more
3. **Essential connection (*väsentlig anknytning*)** to Sweden — applies to former residents

A person who meets **none** of these three grounds is *begränsat skattskyldig* — taxed only on Swedish-source income. This is the correct status for the taxpayer under analysis. They left Sweden in early 2023, do not live in Sweden, have no habitual stay there, and — as analyzed below — lack *väsentlig anknytning* on any material factor.[^3]

***

## 2. The Väsentlig Anknytning Test (3 kap. 7 § IL)

*Väsentlig anknytning* (essential connection) is the critical battleground for departing Swedish residents. Under 3 kap. 7 § IL, Skatteverket evaluates a non-exhaustive list of connection factors:[^4][^5]

| Connection Factor | Taxpayer's Status |
|---|---|
| **Year-round dwelling in Sweden** | **None** — no property, no real estate |
| **Family (spouse/minor children) in Sweden** | **None** |
| **Business operations in Sweden** | **None** |
| **Assets giving influence over Swedish business** | **None** |
| **Swedish citizenship** | Present — creates 5-year presumption (see §3) |
| **Duration of prior Swedish residence** | Unknown, but left in 2023 |
| **Swedish pension entitlements** | Unknown but likely minimal; not a decisive factor |
| **Swedish bank account** | Possibly present — weak indicator (see below) |

The three factors consistently weighted most heavily in case law are: (1) year-round dwelling, (2) family, and (3) significant economic interest in Swedish business. All three are absent for this taxpayer. Swedish courts and Skatterättsnämnden have repeatedly ruled that having only peripheral ties — even multiple ones — does not create *väsentlig anknytning* when the core factors are missing.[^6][^7][^8][^9][^5]

**Bank account:** A Swedish bank account contributes marginally to the overall assessment but is categorically insufficient on its own to establish *väsentlig anknytning*. It is a weak, cumulative factor.[^5]

**Personnummer:** The personnummer remains administratively assigned after emigration but is deactivated for tax purposes once deregistration from *Folkbokföringen* is completed. It is not a connection factor under 3 kap. 7 § IL.[^10]

**Citizenship alone:** Swedish citizenship does **not** create *väsentlig anknytning* by itself — it only creates a **rebuttable presumption** during the 5-year window. Case law (see §3) confirms citizenship is never dispositive.[^4][^5]

***

## 3. The 5-Year Presumption and Burden of Proof

Under 3 kap. 7 § IL, **Swedish citizens** (and long-term residents) are subject to a statutory presumption: for the first **5 calendar years** after emigration, Sweden *presumes* they retain *väsentlig anknytning*, and the burden of proof is on the taxpayer to demonstrate that all material ties have been severed. Since the taxpayer left in early 2023, this window runs until early **2028**.[^11][^5]

This does **not** mean they are automatically *obegränsat skattskyldig*. It means that if Skatteverket were to challenge the non-resident status, the taxpayer must be able to prove absence of ties — not the other way around.

**What constitutes sufficient rebuttal:**

- No year-round dwelling in Sweden (confirmed)
- No family remaining in Sweden (confirmed)
- No business operations in Sweden (confirmed)
- No significant economic holdings in Swedish entities (confirmed)
- Deregistration from *Folkbokföringen* (SKV 7665b form — see §19 below)
- Polish tax residency certificate (*hemvistintyg*/*zaświadczenie o miejscu zamieszkania*)
- Evidence of actual residence in Poland (rental agreements, Polish tax returns filed and paid)

For this taxpayer, the case for rebuttal is **strong**. The only remaining tie is citizenship plus a possible bank account — precisely the scenario that courts have consistently found insufficient. The only reason for any risk is the administrative failure to formally deregister (if not yet done), which Skatteverket would view negatively.[^7][^6]

***

## 4. DTT Tie-Breaker (Article 4 — Sweden–Poland DTT)

Even if a theoretical argument could be made that Swedish domestic law creates *väsentlig anknytning*, the **Sweden–Poland DTT (signed 19 November 2004, in force)** provides a definitive tie-breaker under Article 4:[^12]

1. **Permanent home:** The taxpayer has a permanent home in Poland, not in Sweden → Polish resident under DTT
2. **Centre of vital interests:** All economic activity (JDG, client contracts, bank accounts) and personal life is in Poland → Poland
3. **Habitual abode:** Poland (living there since early 2023)
4. **Nationality:** Swedish (backstop, not needed here)

The DTT resolves any dual-residency conflict decisively in Poland's favor. Even under the worst-case domestic analysis, the DTT establishes the taxpayer is a **Polish resident** for treaty purposes.

## 5. Filing Requirements: Are Swedish Returns Required?

### For a *Begränsat Skattskyldig* Person with No Swedish-Source Income

Skatteverket's own guidance is clear: non-residents are obligated to file only if they have **Swedish-source income**. A *begränsat skattskyldig* person with zero Swedish-source income has **no statutory filing obligation**.[^13][^14][^15]

The trigger conditions for filing under Swedish law are:
- Swedish employment income (work physically performed in Sweden)
- Swedish rental income
- Swedish business income through a permanent establishment (PE) in Sweden
- Capital gains on Swedish *delägarrätter* (shares in Swedish companies) under the 10-year rule
- Swedish pension income
- Capital income (interest, dividends) from Swedish sources of SEK 200 or more[^13]

**None of these apply to this taxpayer.** Therefore:

| Year | Swedish Filing Obligation? | Reasoning |
|------|--------------------------|-----------|
| 2023 | **No** | No Swedish-source income; *begränsat skattskyldig* |
| 2024 | **No** | No Swedish-source income; *begränsat skattskyldig* |
| 2025 | **No** | No Swedish-source income; *begränsat skattskyldig*; JDG has no Polish-to-Swedish connection |

### What If Skatteverket Sends a Pre-Filled Return (*Förifylld Självdeklaration*)?

If the taxpayer has not formally deregistered from *Folkbokföringen*, Skatteverket may still send a pre-filled return under the assumption they remain a Swedish resident. If received:

- **Recommended response:** Submit a brief written response (or the pre-filled form with corrections) asserting *begränsat skattskyldig* status, reporting zero Swedish-source income, and attaching the Polish tax residency certificate
- **Ignoring it entirely** creates a risk that Skatteverket issues a *skönstaxering* (estimated tax assessment) based on assumed residency
- **Filing it as a full worldwide-income return** would incorrectly assert *obegränsad skattskyldighet* and trigger unnecessary Swedish tax claims on income already taxed in Poland

## 6. Income Analysis: Is Any of This Taxpayer's Income Swedish-Source?

### USDC Payments from Byte Masons LLC / Conclave (2023–2025 pre-JDG)

This is compensation for **services rendered in Poland**, paid by a **US company**, received while the taxpayer was **domiciled in Poland**. Under DTT Article 14 (*Independent Personal Services*):[^12]

> *"Income derived by an individual who is a resident of a Contracting State in respect of professional services or other activities of an independent character shall be taxable **only in that State** unless he has a fixed base regularly available to him in the other Contracting State..."*

The taxpayer has **no fixed base in Sweden** — they work from Poland. Therefore, 100% of this income is taxable **only in Poland**. Sweden has **zero taxing rights**.

### JDG Income from Clearstar (2025, May–December)

The Polish JDG is a Polish legal entity invoicing a client (Clearstar) for services rendered from Poland. Under DTT Article 7 (*Business Profits*):[^12]

> *"The profits of an enterprise of a Contracting State shall be taxable only in that State unless the enterprise carries on business in the other Contracting State through a permanent establishment situated therein."*

The JDG has no PE in Sweden, no clients in Sweden, no operations in Sweden. Business profits are taxable **only in Poland**. Sweden has **zero taxing rights**.

### Crypto Trading on Binance and Kraken (2023–2025)

Crypto disposals are covered under DTT Article 13 (*Capital Gains*). The relevant paragraph is **Article 13(4)**:[^12]

> *"Gains from the alienation of **any property other than that referred to in paragraphs (1), (2) and (3)**, shall be taxable **only in the Contracting State of which the alienator is a resident**."*

Crypto assets are not immovable property (Art. 13(1)), not PE-related movable property (Art. 13(2)), and not ships/aircraft (Art. 13(3)). Therefore, crypto gains fall into the **Art. 13(4) residual category** → taxable **only in Poland**. Sweden has **zero taxing rights**.

**Article 13(5) — the 10-year exception:** The DTT does contain a clause (Art. 13(5)) permitting the former state (Sweden) to tax capital gains on "any property" within 10 years of departure. However, this clause **merely preserves treaty permission** — it does not override Swedish domestic law. For Sweden to actually impose tax, it must do so under **3 kap. 19 § IL**, which is expressly limited to *delägarrätter* (equity rights/shares). Crypto is not a *delägarrätt* (see §7 below), so the domestic enabling provision does not exist. Art. 13(5) is therefore inoperative for crypto.[^16][^17][^5]

***

## 7. The 10-Year Rule (*Tioårsregeln*) and Cryptocurrency

### Statutory Scope of 3 kap. 19 § IL

The 10-year rule in 3 kap. 19 § IL extends Swedish tax jurisdiction to capital gains on *delägarrätter* (equity participations) and similar rights issued by Swedish entities, for up to 10 years after the individual ceases to be a Swedish resident. Its explicit scope:[^18][^5][^16]

- Shares (*aktier*) in Swedish *aktiebolag*
- Participations in Swedish partnerships (*HB, KB*)
- Other securities (*värdepapper*) issued by Swedish entities
- Options and rights related to Swedish shares[^5]

### Cryptocurrency is NOT a Delägarrätt

The Swedish Supreme Administrative Court (*Högsta Förvaltningsdomstolen*, HFD) ruled definitively in **HFD 2018 ref. 72** that Bitcoin — and by extension all comparable cryptocurrencies — is **not** a *delägarrätt*, not a foreign currency, and not a personal asset. Bitcoin was classified as an *annan tillgång* ("other asset") under **52 kap. IL**. This classification was upheld in subsequent scholarship and Skatteverket guidance.[^19][^20][^21][^17][^22]

This means:
1. Crypto is taxed under general capital asset rules (*52 kap. IL*), not the equity-rights framework
2. The 10-year rule in 3 kap. 19 § IL **explicitly does not apply** to *övriga tillgångar* (other assets)
3. USDC, USDT, ETH, BTC, DOT — all classified as *övriga tillgångar* → **outside the tioårsregeln**

**Key case reference:** Skatterättsnämnden *Dnr 73-08/D* (2009-04-02), affirmed by HFD *mål nr 2662-09* (2010-12-14): a person who had moved from Sweden to Thailand and sold shares in a *foreign* company was NOT taxable under the 10-year rule because the shares were in a non-Swedish company. The principle is the same here — crypto assets are neither Swedish nor qualify as *delägarrätter*.[^23]

**Conclusion:** The 10-year rule does not apply to any of the taxpayer's crypto holdings or trading activity. Period.

## 8. Risk Assessment by Scenario

### Scenario A: Not Filing / Filing Zero with No Swedish Income (Recommended)

**Legal basis:** Fully defensible. A *begränsat skattskyldig* person with no Swedish-source income has no filing obligation. The DTT assigns all taxing rights to Poland. None of the taxpayer's income has any Swedish connection.[^14][^13]

**Risk of Skatteverket challenge:** Low, provided:
- The taxpayer has or promptly registers the emigration (SKV 7665b)
- Polish taxes are paid and documented
- Polish tax residency certificate (*zaświadczenie o miejscu zamieszkania dla celów podatkowych*) is obtained

**What Skatteverket would need to prove to impose Swedish tax:** They would need to establish *väsentlig anknytning* despite no property, no family, and no business in Sweden — an extremely difficult case given existing case law. The only potential argument would be failure to formally deregister, which creates an evidentiary presumption but is not conclusive.

**Risk level: LOW** — provided administrative steps are taken promptly.

### Scenario B: Filing Worldwide Income to Sweden

**Legal consequence:** This would be legally incorrect. It would assert *obegränsad skattskyldighet* when the taxpayer is properly *begränsat skattskyldig*, and it would expose income already fully taxed in Poland to potential double taxation. While the DTT contains credit-method relief (Art. 22(2)(a)) allowing Sweden to credit Polish taxes paid, this approach:[^12]
- Creates an unnecessary Swedish tax filing obligation going forward
- May trigger scrutiny of all prior years
- Does not benefit the taxpayer in any way (income is already taxed in Poland)
- Creates risk of disputes over valuation, exchange rates, and calculation methodology

**Risk level: HIGH** (pointless risk, zero benefit).

### Scenario C: Previously Missed Filings (2023 and 2024)

**The key question is whether there was a filing obligation at all.** If the taxpayer is correctly *begränsat skattskyldig* with no Swedish-source income, there was no obligation to file for 2023 or 2024. Not filing when there is no obligation is not a violation — there are no penalties for not filing when you are not required to file.

However, if Skatteverket sent a pre-filled return and the taxpayer ignored it without ever deregistering from *Folkbokföringen*, Skatteverket may have issued an automatic assessment treating them as a resident. In that case:

**Correction process (Frivillig Rättelse / Voluntary Disclosure):**
- Sweden has a formal voluntary correction (*frivillig rättelse*) mechanism, analogous to Poland's *czynny żal*[^24][^25][^26]
- If Skatteverket has not yet initiated an investigation, a voluntary correction eliminates *skattetillägg* (tax surcharge) entirely[^26]
- The voluntary correction can cover the **last 6 income years** (i.e., from 2020 onward as of 2026)[^25][^24]
- The taxpayer would file a correction letter asserting *begränsat skattskyldig* status, explaining emigration in early 2023, attaching Polish tax residency documentation and confirmation of Polish tax payments

**Risk level for missed filings (2023–2024): LOW to MODERATE** — the risk depends on whether Skatteverket has already issued assessments. A voluntary correction resolves this cleanly.

## 10. If Reporting Were Required: Forms and Exchange Rates

Although not applicable in this case, for completeness:

**Income reporting forms:**
- **Inkomstdeklaration 1** — main personal income tax return form
- **K4 (Bilaga K4)** — for capital gains and losses from securities and other assets (crypto would go here, under "övriga tillgångar")
- **Income of service (Tjänst)** section of Inkomstdeklaration 1 — for USDC received as payment for services

**Exchange rates — SEK conversion:**
- Riksbanken publishes annual and monthly average exchange rates (*genomsnittskurser*) at riksbank.se[^32][^33][^34]
- For Swedish tax purposes, the **transaction-date spot rate** is technically correct
- In practice, Skatteverket accepts **monthly average rates** for frequent small transactions
- Annual averages are available at riksbank.se/en-gb/statistics/interest-rates-and-exchange-rates/search-annual-and-monthly-average-exchange-rates/[^33][^34]

**USDC received as service payment — Swedish classification:**
Under Swedish law, cryptocurrency received as compensation for services performed is classified as **income of service (*tjänsteinkomst*)** in the hands of the recipient, valued in SEK at the market rate on the date of receipt. A second taxable event occurs when the USDC is later sold (capital gain/loss on the *övrig tillgång*). However, for a *begränsat skattskyldig* person performing services entirely in Poland for a US client, neither the service income nor the subsequent capital gain has a Swedish source.[^35][^36]

## 11. Practical Steps — What to Do Right Now

### Immediate Priority (Most Urgent)

**Step 1: File the Emigration Notification (SKV 7665b) — If Not Yet Done**

This is the single most important administrative step. Submit form **SKV 7665b** (*Anmälan – Flyttning från Sverige*) to Skatteverket, reporting the date of departure (early 2023). This:[^37][^38][^39]
- Deregisters the taxpayer from *Folkbokföringen* (Swedish Population Register)
- Establishes the official record that the taxpayer left Sweden
- Weakens any *väsentlig anknytning* argument Skatteverket could raise
- Can be done online (e-service, requires Swedish BankID) or via paper form sent to Skatteverkets inläsningscentral, FE 2001, 839 86 Östersund

**Note:** There is no time limit for filing this retroactively. Filing it late is better than not filing it at all.

**Step 2: Obtain Polish Tax Residency Certificate**

Request a *zaświadczenie o miejscu zamieszkania dla celów podatkowych* (certificate of tax residence) from the Polish Urząd Skarbowy. This is the Polish equivalent of a *hemvistintyg*. Present this to Skatteverket as documentation of Polish tax residency if requested. The certificate confirms that Poland treats the taxpayer as a resident under the DTT.[^40]

**Step 3: Assess Whether Skatteverket Has Already Issued Assessments**

If the taxpayer was still registered in *Folkbokföringen* and Skatteverket sent pre-filled returns for 2023 and 2024 that were ignored, Skatteverket may have issued automatic *skönstaxering* (estimated assessments). Check by logging into *Mina sidor* (My Pages) at skatteverket.se using Swedish e-identification.

**Step 4: Voluntary Correction (Frivillig Rättelse) if Assessments Exist**

If assessments exist for 2023 and/or 2024 treating the taxpayer as *obegränsat skattskyldig*:[^24][^26]
- File a voluntary correction (*frivillig rättelse*) asserting *begränsat* status
- Attach: (a) proof of emigration date, (b) Polish tax residency certificate, (c) copies of Polish PIT returns showing taxes paid
- The voluntary correction eliminates *skattetillägg* exposure
- Recommended to engage a Swedish tax advisor (*skatterådgivare*) for this step

### Medium-Term Steps

**Step 5: Do Not File 2025 Swedish Return**

For 2025, no Swedish income tax return is required. Do not file Inkomstdeklaration 1 for 2025. If Skatteverket sends a 2025 pre-filled return in spring 2026, respond with a brief letter asserting *begränsat skattskyldig* status and zero Swedish-source income.

**Step 6: Keep the Swedish Bank Account or Close It?**

The bank account does not create *väsentlig anknytning* on its own, so keeping it is permissible. However, from a document-trail perspective, closing it removes even the marginal ambiguity it creates. If closed, ensure funds are transferred to a Polish or international account before closure.[^5]

**Step 7: Forhandsbesked — Not Recommended for This Case**

Applying for a *förhandsbesked* (advance ruling) from Skatterättsnämnden is a formal, expensive process (legal fees typically SEK 20,000–50,000+) suited for genuinely ambiguous situations. This case is not sufficiently ambiguous to justify it — the taxpayer has no property, no family, and no business in Sweden. The legal position is clear. A *förhandsbesked* would be overkill.[^6]

## 13. Risk Flags and Open Issues

**Flag 1 — Deregistration Status (HIGH URGENCY)**
If the taxpayer has not yet filed SKV 7665b, this is the highest-priority action. Skatteverket may still believe the taxpayer is a Swedish resident, may have sent returns, may have issued assessments, and may eventually query the taxpayer about unreported foreign income. Filing the deregistration now, retroactively to 2023, is the correct and legal remedy.

**Flag 2 — Possible Existing Skatteverket Assessments (MODERATE URGENCY)**
If returns were sent and ignored for 2023–2024, check *Mina sidor*. If automatic assessments were issued, engage a Swedish tax advisor promptly and file a voluntary correction. The 6-year window means corrections for 2020–2025 are possible.[^24]

**Flag 3 — The DTT Art. 13(5) "Any Property" Language (LOW RISK)**
Article 13(5) of the Sweden–Poland DTT does use the phrase "any property" which is broader than the domestic Swedish 10-year rule. A theoretical argument could be made that the DTT preserves Sweden's right to tax crypto gains within 10 years. However, this argument fails because: (a) Swedish domestic law has no mechanism to tax *övriga tillgångar* under a 10-year extended liability rule, and (b) Art. 13(5) is a permissive clause ("may be taxed") not a mandatory one, requiring domestic implementing law. The risk is academic, not practical.[^16][^12]

**Flag 4 — Future Changes to Swedish Law**
There is ongoing legislative discussion in Sweden about taxing *övriga tillgångar* (including crypto) under an exit or 10-year framework. As of April 2026, no such change has been enacted. The taxpayer should monitor developments, particularly regarding the government inquiry *Dir. 2022:45* on taxation of unrealized gains upon departure.[^41]

This analysis is intended as legal research, not as formal legal advice. For implementation, consultation with a qualified Swedish *skatterådgivare* is recommended, particularly for the voluntary correction process if Skatteverket assessments exist.
