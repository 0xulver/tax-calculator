## Bottom line

On the facts you gave, the strongest Swedish-law position is that you became **begränsat skattskyldig** in Sweden when you genuinely moved to Poland in early 2023. Under 3 kap. 3 § IL, Sweden keeps someone **obegränsat skattskyldig** only if they are still resident in Sweden, habitually present there, or have **väsentlig anknytning** after previously living there. The statutory tie factors in 3 kap. 7 § are mainly things like a year-round Swedish home, family in Sweden, Swedish business activity, significant influence in Swedish business through assets, Swedish real property, and similar circumstances. ([Riksdagen][1])

With only Swedish citizenship, a possible Swedish bank account, and a personnummer—but **no** Swedish home, family, real estate, business, employer, or material business holdings in Sweden—the facts point strongly **against** väsentlig anknytning. Swedish citizenship is only one factor and recent Skatterättsnämnden decisions say it normally has **less** weight; they also repeat the preparatory-works point that **pure capital placements** should not drive the väsentlig anknytning analysis. ([Skatterättsnämnden][2])

That said, because you are a Swedish citizen and left in early 2023, you are still inside the **five-year presumption** window until early 2028: during that period, **you** must be able to show that you no longer have väsentlig anknytning. Skatteverket states this explicitly. ([Riksdagen][1])

If you are begränsat skattskyldig and have **no Swedish-source income**, the legal baseline is that you generally **do not have to file a Swedish income tax return**. Limited taxpayers are taxed only on the Swedish-source categories listed in 3 kap. 18–19 §§ IL, and Skatteverket expressly says that a limited taxpayer living abroad should **not** declare income from countries other than Sweden. ([Riksdagen][1])

Your described income streams—US clients paying in USDC while you live in Poland, a Polish sole proprietorship billing in EUR, and crypto trading on Binance/Kraken—do **not** fall into those Swedish-source categories on the facts given, and the Sweden–Poland treaty points taxing rights to **Poland**, absent a Swedish permanent establishment, fixed base, Swedish workdays, or Swedish real property. ([Riksdagen][3])

The Swedish **10-year rule** does **not** apply to your crypto. In domestic law, 3 kap. 19 § IL reaches gains on **delägarrätter** and certain partnership interests; 48 kap. 2 § defines delägarrätter as shares and similar equity-type rights. Crypto is treated by Skatteverket and HFD as **andra tillgångar** under 52 kap., not as delägarrätter. ([Riksdagen][1])

## 1. Swedish tax status after the move

### Most likely status: **begränsat skattskyldig**

That is the best reading of the facts you supplied. Unlimited Swedish tax liability after emigration would require Sweden to show one of the 3 kap. 3 § hooks, and the only plausible hook here would be **väsentlig anknytning**. The heavy tie factors listed in 3 kap. 7 § are absent on your facts. ([Riksdagen][1])

### What the väsentlig anknytning test looks at

The statute lists these factors: Swedish citizenship, how long you lived in Sweden, whether you are permanently settled on a specific foreign place, whether you are abroad for studies or health reasons, whether you keep a Swedish year-round home, whether your family remains in Sweden, whether you conduct business in Sweden, whether you hold assets giving substantial influence in Swedish business, whether you own Swedish real property, and similar circumstances. Skatteverket also stresses that this is an **overall** assessment and that sometimes even one factor can carry major weight. ([Riksdagen][1])

### Citizenship, bank account, personnummer

**Citizenship alone** does not normally create väsentlig anknytning. It is a listed factor, but SRN says it usually has **less significance** than stronger ties like a retained permanent home or ongoing Swedish business activity. ([Riksdagen][1])

A plain **Swedish bank account** is not one of the statutory factors, and the preparatory-works line quoted by SRN is that **pure capital placements** should not affect the assessment. That makes a normal bank account a very weak tie on current materials. I did not find a primary source treating a mere bank account, by itself, as enough for väsentlig anknytning. ([Skatterättsnämnden][4])

A **personnummer** also does not make you tax-resident. Skatteverket says you keep your personnummer after emigration, and even a limited taxpayer who has something to declare can be assigned a separate **GD-nummer** despite already having a personnummer. That makes personnummer an administrative identifier, not a residence test. ([Skatteverket][5])

### What you need to show during the five-year presumption period

You do not need a special ruling just to “become” begränsat skattskyldig. What you need is evidence that rebuts the statutory presumption: a real move to Poland, stable home/address there, Polish tax residence, no Swedish year-round home, no Swedish family unit, no Swedish business, no Swedish PE, no major Swedish business holdings, and no habitual presence in Sweden. Skatteverket’s own emigration guidance draws the line this way. ([Skatteverket][6])

A recent SRN decision is a useful contrast: in 2026 it treated a **Swedish sole proprietorship** as a strong tie that kept a person essentially connected to Sweden until the sole proprietorship was deregistered; after deregistration, no väsentlig anknytning remained. You say you have **no Swedish business at all**, which is materially better. ([Skatterättsnämnden][7])

### Is utflyttning registration required?

For **folkbokföring**, yes: if you plan to live abroad for a year or more, you are supposed to notify Skatteverket, via e-service or form **SKV 7665**. But Skatteverket also says tax residence and population registration are **separate systems**, so failure to notify does not by itself make you tax-resident in Sweden. It does, however, make the evidence and administration messier. ([Skatteverket][5])

## 2. Filing obligation for 2023, 2024, 2025

### Legal baseline

Under the Tax Procedure Act, a limited taxpayer files an income tax return if their Swedish taxable or chargeable income reaches **100 SEK**. The same law says that when filing duty depends on an income threshold, you disregard income that is tax-free or that the person is **not taxable for under the Income Tax Act**. ([Riksdagen][8])

Skatteverket’s own page for limited taxpayers abroad says they **should not declare** income from countries other than Sweden. It separately lists the types of Swedish situations that do trigger a declaration: Swedish real property, taxable 10-year-rule share sales, Swedish HB/KB interests, business from a Swedish PE, and certain recaptures. ([Skatteverket][9])

### What that means for your years

Assuming the income you listed was all earned **after the move** and you had **no Swedish-source income**, the best answer is:

* **2023:** no Swedish filing duty for that post-move income, unless Skatteverket actually treated you as still unlimitedly liable or sent you a return that needs to be corrected. ([Riksdagen][8])
* **2024:** same answer. ([Riksdagen][8])
* **2025:** same answer. ([Riksdagen][8])

## 3. Zero filing vs no filing vs full worldwide filing

### Scenario A — no filing / “zero” approach

If you are genuinely begränsat skattskyldig and have **no Swedish-source income**, then **not filing** is the natural legal position. Filing a proactive Swedish “zero return” is not required by the primary sources I found. The caveat is the five-year presumption: if Skatteverket asks, you must be able to prove the lack of väsentlig anknytning. ([Riksdagen][1])

### If Skatteverket sends a pre-filled return

Do **not** ignore it. Skatteverket’s public guidance says: **“Om du har fått en inkomstdeklaration ska du deklarera.”** The law also says Skatteverket sends these notifications to people who can be **assumed** to be declarationally liable, and a declarationally liable person must approve or correct the notified data. The safest practical response is to file a short correction stating that you are **begränsat skattskyldig** and had **no income covered by 3 kap. 18–19 §§ IL** for that year. ([Skatteverket][10])

### Scenario B — filing full worldwide income in Sweden

That is **not** the recommended route on these facts. It risks inviting Sweden to process you as though you were still **obegränsat skattskyldig**. If Sweden did that, the treaty and the implementing law should still protect you: the treaty assigns business/independent-services/most employment income and ordinary capital gains to the residence state unless there is a Swedish PE/fixed base/work state, and the implementing law says income taxable **only in Poland** is not included in Swedish assessment. If Sweden had residual taxing rights, Article 22 provides a foreign tax credit mechanism. ([Riksdagen][3])

### Risk ranking

The lowest-risk practical route is:

1. **No proactive Swedish filing** if no Swedish-source income and no Swedish declaration was issued. ([Riksdagen][8])
2. **Short corrective filing** if a Swedish declaration was issued. ([Skatteverket][10])
3. **Do not file worldwide income as Swedish-taxable income** unless Skatteverket has already taken a contrary position and you are explicitly fighting that issue. ([Skatteverket][6])

## 4. Consequences if 2023/2024 Swedish returns were ignored

If you were actually required to file, or if Skatteverket sent a declaration and then treated you as declarationally liable, the main administrative risks are:

* **Late-filing fee (förseningsavgift):** 1,250 SEK, plus another 1,250 SEK after three months, plus another 1,250 SEK after five months. ([Skatteverket][11])
* **Estimated assessment / skönsbeskattning** and possibly **skattetillägg** if no declaration is filed. Skatteverket says skattetillägg can arise both from incorrect information and from estimated assessment; the usual surcharge is **40%** of the tax that would otherwise have been avoided. ([Skatteverket][12])
* If skattetillägg was imposed because you did not file, it can be removed if you submit a **full declaration within four months** from the end of the month of the decision. ([Skatteverket][12])

Sweden does have a functional equivalent to Poland’s **czynny żal**: **rätta på eget initiativ**. Skatteverket says self-correction can go back **six years** (so, in 2026, back to income year 2020), and it explains that the correction should clearly state that you are correcting the prior incorrect information and changing the assessment, with year-by-year details. There is also a standard **omprövning** route up to six years back. ([Skatteverket][13])

## 5. What Sweden can still tax if you are non-resident

For a **begränsat skattskyldig** person, Sweden taxes only the categories enumerated in 3 kap. 18–19 §§ IL, which in practice means Swedish-source employment/SINK-type income, Swedish artist income, income from a Swedish PE or Swedish real property, certain recaptures, some Swedish-housing income/gains, dividends on Swedish economic associations, and the domestic 10-year rule for shares and certain partnership interests. ([Riksdagen][1])

None of your listed income fits those categories on the facts given:

* US clients paying you in **USDC** for work done while living in Poland: not Swedish-source, no Swedish employer, no Swedish PE or fixed base. Under the treaty, that income is taxable only in the residence state unless there is a Swedish fixed base or Swedish work-state nexus. ([Riksdagen][3])
* Polish **JDG** invoicing foreign clients in EUR: Article 7 allocates business profits to the residence state unless there is a Swedish PE. ([Riksdagen][3])
* Crypto trading on **Binance/Kraken**: not a Swedish-source category under 3 kap. 18 §, and treaty Article 13(4) sends gains on “other property” to the residence state. ([Riksdagen][1])

The only treaty clause that superficially points back toward Sweden is Article 13(5), the former-residence 10-year clause. But treaty clauses do not create domestic tax by themselves; Sweden still needs a domestic charging rule. For crypto, Sweden does **not** have one. ([Riksdagen][3])

## 6. The 10-year rule and crypto

This point is strong.

Sweden’s domestic 10-year rule in **3 kap. 19 § IL** applies to gains on **delägarrätter** under **48 kap. 2 §**, and to interests in Swedish partnerships and foreign transparent entities. The statutory definition of delägarrätt is shares, subscription rights, fund units, interests in economic associations, and similar equity-type instruments. ([Riksdagen][1])

Crypto is not in that bucket. Skatteverket’s crypto page says bitcoin and similar cryptoassets are taxed as **andra tillgångar**, and HFD 2018 ref. 72 says disposals of bitcoin are taxed under **52 kap. 3 §** as “andra tillgångar,” not as delägarrätter. Skatteverket’s page also expressly says crypto is taxed as “andra tillgångar” and **not** as delägarrätter or fordringsrätter. ([Skatteverket][14])

So the answer to question 11 is: **yes, confirmed** — the Swedish domestic 10-year rule does **not** apply to crypto. ([Riksdagen][1])

## 7. If Sweden ever requires reporting anyway

### On your facts, probably nothing

If you remain on the correct **begränsat skattskyldig / no Swedish-source income** footing, then there should be **nothing to report** in Sweden for these income streams. ([Skatteverket][9])

### If a Swedish declaration arrives and you reply

A short note in **Övriga upplysningar** is the cleanest response. Something like:

> Utflyttad från Sverige [datum]. Skatterättsligt hemvist i Polen. Begränsat skattskyldig i Sverige enligt 3 kap. 17 § IL. Ingen väsentlig anknytning enligt 3 kap. 7 § IL. Inga inkomster som omfattas av 3 kap. 18–19 §§ IL under inkomståret [år].

That is a practical drafting suggestion based on the statutes and Skatteverket’s published filing structure. ([Riksdagen][1])

### If Sweden were to treat you as unlimitedly liable

Then foreign income would be reported in **SEK** at the proper place in the return, and you would also give the country and SEK amount in **Övriga upplysningar**. If foreign tax credit is relevant, that is also entered in SEK. Skatteverket says even treaty-exempt foreign income should still be disclosed in a Swedish return **if** you are filing as someone taxable on foreign income in Sweden. ([Skatteverket][15])

For foreign salary/service income in foreign currency, Skatteverket says to use the **payment-date** exchange rate; if you converted the money the same day, use the actual rate; otherwise use the **Riksbanken fixing rate** for the payment date. Annual average rates are acceptable only for stable periodic payments. Riksbanken publishes daily, monthly, and annual averages. ([Skatteverket][16])

For crypto, if Sweden were taxing it, it would generally go on **K4, avsnitt D**. Skatteverket’s crypto page shows that many crypto transactions do **not** always need to be entered one by one: you can aggregate the total proceeds and total cost basis for all gain transactions, and separately for loss transactions, in K4 avsnitt D. That is still **not** a single net annual number. ([Skatteverket][14])

For **USDC compensation**, Swedish guidance treats the receipt as income for the work itself at market value when received; the crypto then has an acquisition value equal to that amount, and later price changes are taxed on disposal. If received in employment, it is tjänsteinkomst; if received in business, later value changes are normally capital unless the crypto is inventory. ([Skatteverket][14])

## 8. What to do right now

The urgent items are these:

* **Check whether Skatteverket actually issued 2023/2024 declarations or decisions.** If yes, do not leave them unresolved. File a correction or request omprövning now. ([Skatteverket][10])
* **Register or regularize your utflyttning** if you never did it. Skatteverket requires notification for moves abroad of one year or more, via e-service or **SKV 7665**. ([Skatteverket][5])

That is the practical way to rebut the 3 kap. 7 § presumption. ([Riksdagen][1])
* **Optionally get a Polish certificate of residence.** It is not mandatory in ordinary cases, but it is useful evidence if Skatteverket questions your residence or treaty position. Poland issues it on **CFR-1 / WN-CFR**, and the Polish tax portal says it is used for international tax settlements and treaty application. ([Podatki][17])
* **Do not apply for a förhandsbesked unless there is an active dispute or you want formal certainty at real cost.** Skatterättsnämnden says the issue must be concrete and properly documented; for individuals the fee is typically around **6,000 SEK** and can be up to **20,000 SEK**. For income tax, the application must be filed no later than the tax return due date for the year concerned. On your fact pattern, that is usually overkill. ([Skatterättsnämnden][18])
